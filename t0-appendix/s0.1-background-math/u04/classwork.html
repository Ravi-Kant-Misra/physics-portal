Part D — Short Answer

11. The unit of momentum is kg·m·s⁻¹. The unit of force is kg·m·s⁻². Explain in one sentence why force has an extra s⁻¹ compared to momentum.
12. A student says "the Joule and the Newton·meter are different units." Are they correct? Show by reducing N·m to fundamental units.
13. Verify that kg·m²·s⁻³·A⁻² (the Ohm) can also be obtained from R = P/I², using W = kg·m²·s⁻³. Show full working.

Answer Key

Part A — Step-by-Step

Q1 — Newton
N = kg × m·s⁻² = kg·m·s⁻²
F = ma; a has units m·s⁻²
Q2 — Pascal
Pa = kg·m·s⁻² / m² = kg·m⁻¹·s⁻²
F(kg·m·s⁻²) ÷ Area(m²)
Q3 — Joule (via Eₖ)
v² = m²·s⁻²; J = kg·m²·s⁻²
½ is dimensionless; kg × m²·s⁻²
Q4 — Watt
W = kg·m²·s⁻² / s = kg·m²·s⁻³
s⁻²÷s = s⁻²⁻¹ = s⁻³

Part B — Direct Reduction

Q5 — Volt
kg·m²·s⁻³·A⁻¹
V = W/A = kg·m²·s⁻³ / A
Q6 — Ohm
kg·m²·s⁻³·A⁻²
R = V/I = kg·m²·s⁻³·A⁻¹ / A

Part D — Short Answer

Q11: Force = rate of change of momentum; dividing momentum (kg·m·s⁻¹) by time (s) adds one more s⁻¹, giving kg·m·s⁻².
Q12: The student is wrong — they are the same unit. N·m = (kg·m·s⁻²)·m = kg·m²·s⁻², which equals 1 Joule. Work = force × distance.
Q13: R = P/I² → W/A² = (kg·m²·s⁻³) / A² = kg·m²·s⁻³·A⁻². This matches the result from R = V/I, confirming both routes give the same fundamental units.
