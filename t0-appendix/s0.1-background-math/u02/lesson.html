Calculating with Significant Figures

How to keep the right number of sig figs when you add, subtract, multiply, or divide

🤔

Why Calculations Need Sig Fig Rules

You already know how to count significant figures in a single number. But what happens when you calculate with measurements?

Imagine you measure two lengths with different rulers. One gives you 12.3 cm (3 sig figs, precision ±0.1 cm). Another gives you 4.56 cm (3 sig figs, precision ±0.01 cm). When you add them, does your answer magically become more precise?

No. Your answer can only be as precise as your least precise measurement. The calculation rules exist to enforce this — they stop you from claiming false precision in your results.

➕

Addition & Subtraction

Answer has the same number of decimal places as the measurement with the fewest decimal places.

✖️

Multiplication & Division

Answer has the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest sig figs.

🔢

Powers & Roots

Answer keeps the same number of sig figs as the original number — same rule as multiply/divide.

📋

Addition & Subtraction

Count Decimal Places

Look at each number's decimal places (digits after the decimal point). Your answer must be rounded to match the number with the fewest decimal places.

Why? Because the least precise number only gives information to a certain decimal place. Knowing more decimal places beyond that is meaningless.

12.31 dp
+ 4.562 dp
= 16.86 → 16.9 (1 dp)

Multiplication & Division

Count Sig Figs

Look at each number's total significant figures. Your answer must be rounded to match the number with the fewest sig figs.

Why? Multiplying or dividing two measurements combines their relative uncertainties. The least precise measurement limits how precise the result can be.

4.56 3 sf
× 1.4 2 sf
= 6.384 → 6.4 (2 sf)

xⁿ   √
Powers & Roots

Count Sig Figs (same as × ÷)

Your answer keeps the same number of sig figs as the number you started with.

Why? A power is repeated multiplication; a root is the inverse. Both operations combine the same number's uncertainty with itself — the sig fig count doesn't change.

(2.3)2  2 sf
= 5.29 → 5.3 (2 sf)

√4.56  3 sf
= 2.135… → 2.14 (3 sf)

Mixed operations — do in steps

Rounding Rules

⬇️

Round DOWN

digit < 5

Drop the digit and leave the previous digit unchanged.

3.74 → 3.7 (to 2 sf)

⬆️

Round UP

digit > 5

Drop the digit and add 1 to the previous digit.

3.76 → 3.8 (to 2 sf)

🎲

The "5" case

digit = 5

Round to the nearest even digit. (Banker's rounding — avoids bias.)

3.75 → 3.8  ·  3.65 → 3.6

💡 For most high school problems, just use the simple rule: digit ≥ 5 → round up, digit < 5 → round down. The "round to even" rule matters more in advanced lab work.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Using sig fig rule for + and −

12.1 + 0.045 = 12.1 (1 sf?? wrong!)
12.1 + 0.045 = 12.1 (1 dp ✓)

Addition uses decimal places, not total sig figs. The answer 12.1 has 3 sig figs — that's fine.

❌ Using decimal place rule for × and ÷

3.2 × 1.45 = 4.64 (2 dp? wrong!)
3.2 × 1.45 = 4.6 (2 sf ✓)

Multiplication uses sig figs, not decimal places. 3.2 has 2 sig figs → answer has 2 sig figs.

❌ Rounding intermediate steps

Round at every step → error builds
Keep full digits → round once at end

Always carry full calculator precision through multi-step problems. Round only the final answer.

❌ Forgetting exact numbers have ∞ sig figs

Counting "2 apples" as 1 sig fig
Counted/defined values → unlimited sig figs

Numbers from counting (2 apples, 3 sides) or definitions (1 km = 1000 m) are exact — they never limit your sig figs.
