Review of Trigonometry

Triangle Sides & Trig Identities — SOH CAH TOA

🕒

What is Trigonometry?

Trigonometry is the study of the relationships between the angles and side lengths of triangles.

In IB Physics, we use three key trig functions — Sin, Cos, and Tan — to find unknown side lengths or angles in right triangles. These functions are simply fractions formed by pairing different side lengths.

The most important skill is correctly labelling the sides of a triangle relative to the angle you are using. The labels change depending on which angle you choose!

📌

Labelling the Three Sides

Every side of a right triangle gets a name that depends on which angle (θ) you are working with. Choose an angle (not the right angle), and then label the three sides:

Opposite

The side directly across the triangle from the angle θ. It does not touch θ.

Adjacent

The side that sits right next to the angle θ. It forms one side of the angle.

Hypotenuse

Always the longest side, opposite the 90° right angle. Never changes.

Angle θ at Bottom-Left

Angle θ at Top-Right (same triangle, labels change!)

Key takeaway: The Hypotenuse is always the longest side (opposite the right angle) and never changes. But Opposite and Adjacent swap depending on which non-right angle you call θ. In the 3-4-5 triangle above, moving θ from the bottom-left corner to the top-right corner causes opposite and adjacent to exchange their values.

📈

The Three Trig Identities

Once you have labelled the sides, you can write the three trig ratios. These are always ratios (fractions) of two side lengths.

Sine
Sin(θ) = Opp⁄Hyp
S — O/H
Cosine
Cos(θ) = Adj⁄Hyp
C — A/H
Tangent
Tan(θ) = Opp⁄Adj
T — O/A
SOH
Sin = Opp/Hyp
CAH
Cos = Adj/Hyp
TOA
Tan = Opp/Adj

Mnemonic: SOH–CAH–TOA

Solving for a Missing Side — 3-Step Method

When you know one side and one angle of a right triangle, you can find any other side using trig. Follow these three steps every time.

1
Choose the right trig identity

Look at which sides you know and which side you want. Pick the trig function (Sin, Cos, or Tan) whose formula contains both of those sides.

Know Adj, want Hyp → Cos  |  Know Adj, want Opp → Tan
2
Rearrange the formula

Isolate the unknown side on one side of the equation using algebra (multiply or divide both sides).

Cos(θ) = A/H → H = A / Cos(θ)
3
Substitute and calculate

Plug in the known values and compute. Make sure your calculator is in degree mode!

H = 50 / Cos(15°) = 50 / 0.97 ≈ 51.5

Worked Examples — Finding Missing Sides

Example 5 Find the Hypotenuse (angle = 15°, adjacent = 50)

Given: Angle θ = 15°, Adjacent side = 50

Find: Hypotenuse H

Sides involved: Adjacent and Hypotenuse → use Cosine

1

Choose identity: We know Adjacent (A) and want Hypotenuse (H). Both appear in Cos.

Cos(θ) = A / H

2

Rearrange: Multiply both sides by H, then divide by Cos(θ).

H × Cos(θ) = A   →   H = A / Cos(θ)

3

Substitute: A = 50, θ = 15°, Cos(15°) ≈ 0.966

H = 50 / 0.966 ≈ 51.8

Hypotenuse
H ≈ 51.8
Example 6 Find the Opposite side (angle = 15°, adjacent = 50)

Given: Angle θ = 15°, Adjacent side = 50

Find: Opposite side O

Sides involved: Adjacent and Opposite → use Tangent

1

Choose identity: We know Adjacent (A) and want Opposite (O). Both appear in Tan.

Tan(θ) = O / A

2

Rearrange: Multiply both sides by A.

O = A × Tan(θ)

3

Substitute: A = 50, θ = 15°, Tan(15°) ≈ 0.268

O = 50 × 0.268 ≈ 13.4

Opposite
O ≈ 13.4

Quick-Reference: Choosing the Trig Function

Adjacent & Hypotenuse Cos Cos(θ) = A/H A = H·Cos   or   H = A/Cos
Opposite & Adjacent Tan Tan(θ) = O/A O = A·Tan   or   A = O/Tan

Quick Check Quiz

Test Your Understanding (click an answer)

1. In a right triangle, the angle θ is at the bottom-left corner. Which side is the Adjacent?

Correct! The Adjacent is always the side right next to the angle (forming one side of the angle) — not the hypotenuse.
Not quite. The Adjacent side is the one that sits right next to angle θ (other than the hypotenuse). Review the definitions above.

2. For a 3-4-5 right triangle with θ at the bottom-left (Opp = 3, Adj = 4, Hyp = 5), what is Tan(θ)?

Correct! Tan(θ) = Opp/Adj = 3/4 = 0.75.
Not quite. Tan(θ) = Opposite / Adjacent = 3/4 = 0.75. Remember SOH-CAH-TOA.

3. A right triangle has θ = 30° and Adjacent = 20 m. You want to find the Opposite side. Which step 2 rearrangement is correct?

Correct! Adjacent and Opposite → use Tan. Tan(θ) = O/A, so O = A × Tan(θ) = 20 × Tan(30°) ≈ 11.5 m.
Not quite. When the known side is Adjacent and you want Opposite, use Tangent: Tan(θ) = O/A, so O = A × Tan(θ).
