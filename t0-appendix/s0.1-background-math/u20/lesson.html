Finding the Angle of a Vector

Using inverse trigonometry to find the direction of a resultant vector

🤔

Why Inverse Trig?

When two vectors that point up, down, left, or right are added together, the result (the resultant) forms the hypotenuse of a right triangle. The two original components are the two legs of that triangle.

We already know the lengths of the sides (the component magnitudes). What we want now is the angle — the direction of the resultant. Because we know the sides and need the angle, we use inverse trigonometry: sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, or tan⁻¹.

The angle we report is always measured from the horizontal (the x-axis direction). This is the standard convention in IB Physics.

📝

The Three Inverse Trig Formulas

Inverse Sine
sin⁻¹( opp / hyp ) = θ
Use when you know the side opposite θ and the hypotenuse
Inverse Cosine
cos⁻¹( adj / hyp ) = θ
Use when you know the adjacent side and the hypotenuse
Inverse Tangent
tan⁻¹( opp / adj ) = θ
Use when you know both legs of the triangle (most common!)
🌟

The Golden Rule for Direction Problems

Because the angle is measured from horizontal, the horizontal component = adjacent side and the vertical component = opposite side.

This means the most common formula in IB vector problems is:

θ = tan⁻¹( vertical component / horizontal component )
📏

Right Triangle Reference

Remembering which side is which:

Adjacent — the horizontal leg; lies along or beside the angle θ
Opposite — the vertical leg; faces the angle θ from across the triangle
Hypotenuse — the longest side; the resultant vector (opposite the 90° corner)
✎️

Worked Examples

Example A Inverse Sine — careful choice!
Hypotenuse = 10 Opposite (vertical) = 7
1
We know the hypotenuse and the opposite side, so use sin⁻¹.
2
θ = sin⁻¹(7 / 10)
3
θ = sin⁻¹(0.7) = 44.4°
✓ θ = 44.4°
Example B Inverse Tangent — both legs known
Opposite (vertical) = 4 Adjacent (horizontal) = 5
1
Both legs are given, so use tan⁻¹.
2
θ = tan⁻¹(4 / 5)
3
θ = tan⁻¹(0.8) = 38.7°
✓ θ = 38.7°
Example C Inverse Cosine — adjacent and hypotenuse
Adjacent (horizontal) = 3 Hypotenuse = 3.5
1
We know adjacent and hypotenuse, so use cos⁻¹.
2
θ = cos⁻¹(3 / 3.5)
3
θ = cos⁻¹(0.857) = 31.0°
✓ θ = 31.0°
Example D Inverse Tangent — steep angle
Opposite (vertical) = 6 Adjacent (horizontal) = 3
1
Both legs known → use tan⁻¹.
2
θ = tan⁻¹(6 / 3)
3
θ = tan⁻¹(2) = 63.4°
✓ θ = 63.4°
🌊

Real-World Example: The River Problem

A swimmer crosses a river by swimming straight across at 3 m/s (horizontal). The river current pushes the swimmer downstream at 5 m/s (vertical). What is the magnitude and angle of the resultant velocity?

Step-by-Step Solution

1
Identify components: horizontal = 3 m/s, vertical = 5 m/s
2
Find magnitude with Pythagoras:
R = √(3² + 5²) = √(9 + 25) = √34 ≈ 5.8 m/s
3
Find angle using tan⁻¹:
θ = tan⁻¹(5 / 3) = tan⁻¹(1.667)
4
θ = 59° from horizontal (South of East / downstream)

Resultant velocity

5.8 m/s at 59° South of East
⚠️

Common Mistake to Avoid

❌ Wrong approach: When a vector has components (e.g. horizontal = 17.3, vertical = 10) and you've been given the resultant magnitude (e.g. 20), students sometimes try to use Tan⁻¹(10/17.3). This can work IF the angle is from horizontal — BUT you must make sure you're putting vertical (opposite) on top and horizontal (adjacent) on the bottom.

The real rule: always label your triangle first. Mark which side is opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse relative to the angle you want (measured from horizontal). Then choose the right formula.

Summary

When to use tan⁻¹

Both legs of the triangle are known. tan⁻¹(opp/adj) — most common in IB vector problems.

When to use sin⁻¹

The opposite side and hypotenuse are known. sin⁻¹(opp/hyp)

When to use cos⁻¹

The adjacent side and hypotenuse are known. cos⁻¹(adj/hyp)

Direction Convention

Angle is always measured from horizontal. State direction clearly: e.g. "59° South of East" or "26.6° North of East".
