Variables, Units & Prefixes

Understand how physicists name, measure, and scale every quantity in the universe

🔢

What Are Variables and Units?

📐

Variable

A variable is any quantity that can change its value — speed, temperature, mass, distance, time. In physics, variables are the "what" we are measuring.

A variable is a quantity that can change its value.
📏

Unit

A unit is a definite magnitude or quantity used to measure the same type of thing. Units give meaning to numbers — without them, numbers are just guesses.

A unit is a definite magnitude used to measure a type of quantity.

The Golden Rule

Variable = Number × Unit
Weight = 35 pounds
Time = 20 seconds
Distance = 5 miles

Units give meaning to numbers in equations. For example, saying "I ran for 5" is meaningless — saying "I ran for 5 miles" is complete information.

✖️

Units Behave Like Numbers in Equations

Units written next to numbers are being multiplied by those numbers — just like algebra. They follow all the same rules as multiplying and dividing algebraic variables.

✖️

Multiplying units — they combine

When you multiply quantities, the units multiply too — just like algebraic variables. The units get "glued" together.

Units
10kg × 5m = 50 kg·m
Same as algebra
10x × 5y = 50xy
➗

Dividing units — they cancel or form fractions

When you divide quantities, units divide too. Identical units on top and bottom cancel, others form a fraction unit (like m/s for speed).

Units
10kg ÷ 5m = 2 kg/m
Same as algebra
10x ÷ 5y = 2 x/y
³

Raising units to a power — exponent applies to unit too

When a quantity with a unit is raised to a power, the exponent applies to both the number and the unit.

Units
(10m)³ = 10³ m³ = 1000 m³
Same as algebra
(10x)³ = 10³x³
➕

Adding / Subtracting — units must match!

You can only add or subtract quantities with the same unit. Trying to add kilograms to meters makes no physical sense — just like you can't add x to y in algebra.

✅ OK
10kg + 5kg = 15 kg
❌ Not OK
10kg + 5m ≠ 15 kgm

Writing Units in Fractions (IB Physics Convention)

⚠️ In IB Physics, we do not write units as fractions with numerators/denominators. Instead, we use negative exponents. Reminder: 1/xa = x−a
5 kg · 8 N · m³
─────────────── = 160 kg·N·m³ · m⁻¹ · A⁻² = 16 kg·N·m²·A⁻²
10 m · 1 A²
Units in the denominator become negative exponents in the numerator. This is the standard IB/scientific notation for compound units.

The SI Unit System

SI stands for Système International — French for "The International System." SI units are the internationally agreed-upon system used by all scientists worldwide. They are the modern form of the metric system, designed to be easy to use in science.

SI Units

Length: Meter
Force: Newton
Volume: Liter
Mass: Kilogram
Time: Second
vs.

Imperial Units

Length: Foot
Force: Pound
Volume: Gallon
Mass: Pound (mass)
Time: Second

⭐ The kilogram is the only fundamental SI unit that already has a prefix. Scientists chose "kilogram" instead of "gram" as the base because a kilogram is closer in magnitude to everyday objects — it's about 2.2 pounds.

There are only 7 fundamental SI units. All other units in physics are derived by combining these 7. Units that are combinations of the fundamentals are called derived units.

SI Unit Prefixes

SI units can have prefixes — symbols placed at the beginning of a unit to change its size by a power of 10. Prefixes let us write very large or very small measurements without awkward strings of zeros.

PrefixSymbolMultiplierExample
GigaG10⁹1 GHz = 1,000,000,000 Hz
MegaM10⁶1 MB = 1,000,000 bytes
Kilok10³1 km = 1,000 m
centic10⁻²1 cm = 0.01 m
millim10⁻³1 mm = 0.001 m
microμ10⁻⁶1 μm = 0.000001 m
nanon10⁻⁹1 nm = 0.000000001 m

Special note about the kilogram

The kilogram is the only fundamental SI unit with a prefix already in its name. This means when we add more prefixes to mass, we add them to "gram" (not kilogram): a milligram is mg, not "mkg." One kilogram = 1000 grams = 10³ g.
