Key Reminders

V
Vector — has magnitude AND direction. Drawn as an arrow. Examples: displacement, force, velocity, acceleration, momentum.
S
Scalar — has magnitude ONLY. No direction needed. Examples: length, area, mass, temperature, energy.
!
The arrow length represents magnitude. If magnitude doubles, the arrow doubles. The arrowhead shows direction.

Part C — Short Answers

1. A vector has both magnitude (size) and direction. A scalar has magnitude only.
2. No — they are not the same. Speed is a scalar: it tells how fast an object is moving (e.g. 30 m/s) but says nothing about direction. Velocity is a vector: it includes both speed and direction (e.g. 30 m/s north). This matters when two cars travel at 30 m/s but in opposite directions — their velocities are different even though their speeds are equal.
3. Forces are vectors, so they can cancel or add depending on direction. Without knowing direction, you cannot determine the net force. A 20 N right and 10 N left partially cancel (net = 10 N right); but if both pointed right the net would be 30 N. Direction is essential to find the correct result.

Part D — Sentence Completion

1. Displacement is a vector quantity because it has both magnitude and direction.

2. Mass is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude only and no direction.

3. When drawing a vector, the length of the arrow represents the magnitude, and the arrowhead shows the direction.

4. If you double the magnitude of a force vector, the arrow in the diagram becomes twice as long.

5. Speed is a scalar, but velocity is a vector because it includes direction.
