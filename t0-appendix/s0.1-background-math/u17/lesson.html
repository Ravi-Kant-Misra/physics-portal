Vectors vs. Scalars

Every quantity in physics is either a vector or a scalar — learn to tell them apart

📐

What Is a Physical Quantity?

💡

Key Idea

A physical quantity is any measurable amount of something — speed, mass, temperature, force. Every quantity in physics belongs to one of two families: vectors or scalars. Knowing which family a quantity belongs to tells you how to add it, draw it, and use it in equations.

Vector

A vector has both magnitude (size) and direction.

You cannot describe a vector completely without saying where it points. Vectors are drawn as arrows: the length of the arrow represents the magnitude; the arrowhead shows the direction.
e.g. "5 m east" — magnitude = 5 m, direction = east

Scalar

A scalar has magnitude (size) only — no direction needed.

A single number (with a unit) tells the whole story. Scalars are shown as plain numbers or dotted lines — no arrowhead.
e.g. "5 m" — just a size, no direction
➡️

Vectors Are Drawn as Arrows

Vector: "5 m east"

Arrow length = magnitude (5 m). Arrowhead = direction (east). Both are essential.

Scalar: "5 m"

No arrowhead — scalars only have size. No direction is needed or given.

Doubling the Magnitude = Doubling the Arrow

The arrow is drawn to scale — bigger force means a longer arrow.

📋

Vector & Scalar Quantities — Examples

Quantity Type Why? Example
Displacement Vector Has size AND direction 30 m north
Distance / Length Scalar Just a size — no direction needed 30 m
Velocity Vector Speed in a specific direction 20 m/s east
Speed Scalar How fast — no direction 20 m/s
Acceleration Vector Rate of change of velocity — direction matters 9.8 m/s² downward
Force Vector A push or pull — you must know which way it acts 50 N to the right
Momentum Vector Moving object's "punch" — direction same as velocity 10 kg·m/s west
Mass Scalar Amount of matter — no direction 5 kg
Temperature Scalar Hotness — no direction 25 °C
Energy / Work Scalar Amount of energy — not directional 200 J
Area Scalar Surface measurement — no direction 12 m²

⚠️

Direction Changes Everything

A 30 N push to the right and a 30 N push to the left are NOT the same — they have equal magnitudes but opposite directions. Because force is a vector, direction must always be stated. If you ignore direction, you cannot correctly calculate the motion of an object.

⚡

Quick-Reference Summary

Vectors

  • Magnitude + Direction
  • Drawn as arrows
  • Longer arrow = bigger magnitude
  • Displacement, Velocity, Acceleration
  • Force, Momentum

Scalars

  • Magnitude only — no direction
  • Written as plain numbers
  • No arrowhead needed
  • Distance, Speed, Mass
  • Temperature, Energy, Area
