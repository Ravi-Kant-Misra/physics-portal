Part B — True or False

1. FALSE — Distance is a scalar (magnitude only). Displacement is a vector because it also includes direction (e.g. 5 m north).
2. FALSE — The arrowhead shows the direction of the vector. The arrow's length represents the magnitude.
3. FALSE — They have the same magnitude but opposite directions, so they are different (and opposite) vectors. Equal magnitudes alone are not enough — direction must match for vectors to be identical.
4. TRUE — Arrow length is proportional to magnitude. Halving the magnitude halves the arrow length.
5. FALSE — Energy is a scalar. It has no direction. The ability to exert forces does not make energy itself directional.

Part C — Scenarios

Scenario 1a. "200 km" is a scalar (distance) — only magnitude, no direction. "200 km north-east" is a vector (displacement) — it has both magnitude (200 km) and direction (north-east).
Scenario 1b. The second statement (vector) is more useful. Knowing only the distance covered tells you nothing about the ship's final position. Including direction allows precise navigation and tracking of the ship's location.
Scenario 2a. No — they are not the same vector. Vectors require both magnitude and direction. Both forces have magnitude 80 N, but they point in opposite directions (one east, one west), making them different vectors with opposite senses.
Scenario 3. Wind velocity is a vector; wind speed is a scalar. A meteorologist tracking a storm needs to know not just how fast the wind is moving (speed/scalar) but also where it is heading (direction). Using velocity — a vector — provides both pieces of information, allowing accurate prediction of the storm's path.

Part D — Extended Responses

1. The student's claim is incorrect. Acceleration due to gravity is 9.8 m/s² downward — a vector, not just the number 9.8. The direction "downward" is essential: it explains why objects fall toward Earth and not sideways. If gravity were a scalar, you could not explain why a ball thrown horizontally still curves downward. The direction component is what distinguishes gravitational acceleration (a vector) from, say, the scalar quantity of gravitational potential energy. Removing direction removes critical physical meaning.
2. All three forces are vectors because each has both a magnitude and a direction — gravity acts 10 N downward, normal force acts 10 N upward, and the push acts 5 N to the right. The vertical forces (10 N down and 10 N up) are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, so they cancel — resulting in zero net vertical force. The only unbalanced force is the 5 N push to the right. Therefore, according to Newton's second law, the box accelerates to the right. Without knowing directions, we could not determine this outcome.

Part E — Diagram Analysis

a) Arrows A and C have the same length (same magnitude) but point in opposite directions (A = right, C = left).

b) Arrow B — its length is twice that of A, representing double the magnitude.

c) No — A and C are not the same vector. Although they have the same magnitude, they point in opposite directions. For two vectors to be identical, both magnitude AND direction must match. A points right; C points left — they are opposite vectors.

d) To fully describe arrow D (a force), you must state: (1) the magnitude (e.g. 30 N, read from the scale) and (2) the direction (upward / vertically upward). Both pieces of information are required because force is a vector quantity.
