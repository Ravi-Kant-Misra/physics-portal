Organizing a Data Table

Learn how to build a complete IB Physics data table — with IV, DV trials, averages, statistical uncertainty, and error bars

📋

What Is a Data Table?

A data table is where you record the data from your experiment before displaying it as a graph. In IB Physics, every data table has a specific structure with labeled columns for the independent variable, repeated trials of the dependent variable, averages, statistical uncertainty, and error bars.

A good data table makes your experiment reproducible and your uncertainty transparent. It is the foundation of every IB lab report.

🗂️

Anatomy of an IB Data Table

Standard IB Data Table Structure

Data Table:     Control Values: _______________

IV
±____		 DV: ___
±____		 Average DV Statistical
Uncertainty		 Error Bars
Trial 1 Trial 2 Trial 3

IV Column (blue)

Independent Variable — what you control and change. Label as: variable name / unit. Include the measurement uncertainty (±) in the header.

DV Trials (green)

Dependent Variable — what you measure. At least 3 repeated trials. Label the DV header with name and unit; each trial column just needs "Trial 1", "Trial 2", "Trial 3".

Average DV (yellow)

Mean of the trials: Average = sum of trials ÷ number of trials. This is the value you plot on the graph.

Statistical Uncertainty (orange)

How spread out are the trials? Formula: ±(max − min) / 2. This reflects how repeatable your measurements are.

Error Bars (red)

The larger of the measurement uncertainty and the statistical uncertainty. This is what you actually draw on the graph as error bars.

Control Values

Variables you kept constant throughout. List them above the table. These show the reader you ran a fair test.

🧮

Key Formulas

Data Table Calculations

Average DV

Average = (T1 + T2 + T3) / 3

Statistical Uncertainty

Δ_stat = (max − min) / 2

Error Bar value

Error Bar = larger of (Δ_measurement, Δ_stat)

Column Label Format

Variable name / unit

Row 1 worked
Mass = 0.050 kg — calculating each column

Average: (0.012 + 0.010 + 0.008) / 3 = 0.030 / 3 = 0.010 m

Statistical Uncertainty: (max − min) / 2 = (0.012 − 0.008) / 2 = 0.004 / 2 = ± 0.002 m

Error Bar: Compare measurement uncertainty (± 0.001 m) with statistical uncertainty (± 0.002 m). The larger is 0.002 m, so the error bar = ± 0.002 m.

Why is the error bar the LARGER uncertainty?

The error bar must represent the total uncertainty in your DV. If your measurements scatter more than your instrument precision (statistical > measurement), the scatter dominates. If your instrument is less precise than the scatter (measurement > statistical), the instrument limit dominates. Always use whichever is larger — it's the most honest representation of how uncertain your data point is.

Error Bars & Axis Rules

Error Bar Rule

To find the error bar value, compare the measurement uncertainty and statistical uncertainty of the DV. The larger uncertainty is the error bar value.

Error Bar = max( Δ_measurement , Δ_statistical )

The error bar column in your data table is what you actually draw on the graph for each data point.

X Axis (Horizontal) — Always the IV

The Independent Variable always goes on the x (horizontal) axis. Label each axis with the variable name / unit.

Mass (kg)

Y Axis (Vertical) — Always the DV

The Dependent Variable always goes on the y (vertical) axis. Error bars from your table are plotted on this axis (Y-axis error bars).

Stretch (m)

X-axis vs Y-axis error bars

The IV uncertainty (e.g. ± 0.001 kg on mass) gives you X-axis error bars. The Error Bar column from your table (larger of measurement vs statistical uncertainty of the DV) gives you Y-axis error bars. Both are plotted on your graph. In many experiments X error bars are very small compared to Y error bars.
