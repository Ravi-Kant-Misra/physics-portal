Significant Figures

Master the art of knowing which digits in a number actually count

🤔

Why Do Significant Figures Matter?

Imagine you measure a desk with a regular school ruler. You get 1.2 m. Your friend uses a fancy laser measurer and gets 1.2047 m. Both are "correct" — but they say very different things about how precisely the measurement was made.

The digits you can actually trust based on your instrument are called Significant Figures (sig figs). They tell the reader exactly how good your measurement is — no more, no less.

Writing too many digits is dishonest — it claims more precision than you have. Writing too few throws away real information.

Your sig figs choice tells the story!
🌡️
Temperature
Unit: degrees Celsius (°C) or Kelvin (K)

What is temperature? Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles in an object. The hotter something is, the faster its particles move. We use Celsius (°C) for everyday measurements and Kelvin (K) for scientific calculations.

Why do sig figs matter here? A fever thermometer might be trusted to 0.1°C. A lab thermometer to 0.01°C. Writing more digits than your instrument can read is a lie about your precision.

Same patient, different instruments:
Fever strip37°C→ 2 sig figs — precision to nearest degree
Clinical thermometer36.6°C→ 3 sig figs — precision to nearest tenth
Lab digital probe36.47°C→ 4 sig figs — precision to nearest hundredth
⚠️ Writing 36.470000°C from a basic fever strip is wrong — you're claiming precision you never had!
⚖️
Mass
Unit: grams (g), kilograms (kg), milligrams (mg)

What is mass? Mass measures how much matter is in an object. Unlike weight (which changes depending on gravity), mass stays the same whether you're on Earth, the Moon, or in space.

Why do sig figs matter here? If a pharmacist says a tablet contains 500 mg of medicine, that's ambiguous — is it exactly 500, or somewhere between 450 and 550? Scientific notation clears it up: 5.00 × 10² mg means three sig figs.

Same 250 g object, different scales:
Bathroom scale250 g→ 2–3 sig figs — ambiguous without decimal!
Kitchen scale250. g→ 3 sig figs — decimal confirms precision to 1 g
Analytical balance250.0413 g→ 7 sig figs — precision to 0.0001 g
⚠️ A pharmacist needs the analytical balance result. Sig figs tell you which instrument was used!
📏
Length
Unit: meters (m), centimeters (cm), kilometers (km)

What is length? Length measures how far apart two points are in space. Area and volume are both built from length measurements. Tools range from a simple ruler (to mm) to laser meters (to 0.1 mm).

Why do sig figs matter here? A civil engineer writing 1200 m for a bridge span is dangerously ambiguous. Is it ±50 m or ±0.5 m? For a bridge, that difference could be catastrophic.

Measuring the same 74 mm rod:
Rough tape7 cm→ 1 sig fig — precision ±1 cm
School ruler7.4 cm→ 2 sig figs — precision ±1 mm
Vernier caliper7.40 cm→ 3 sig figs — precision ±0.1 mm
⚠️ For a bridge or satellite, writing the wrong sig figs can mean real engineering errors!

Scientific Notation & Sig Figs

🔑 The Golden Rule

Only the digits in the coefficient count. The power of 10 describes size, not precision.

3.060  ×  104
→
Coefficient 3.060 → 4 sig figs
Power 10⁴ → does NOT count
💡 Pro tip: Instead of writing 5000 (ambiguous), write: 5 × 10³ (1 sf) · 5.0 × 10³ (2 sf) · 5.00 × 10³ (3 sf) · 5.000 × 10³ (4 sf)
🧠

Memory Tricks

🏖️

Pacific / Atlantic Rule

Decimal Present → start from Pacific (left). Decimal Absent → start from Atlantic (right). Count from the first non-zero digit inward.

🥪

The Sandwich Zero

A zero between two non-zero digits is like filling in a sandwich — it's always there, always counts. 403, 5002 — all trapped zeros count.

🔬

Sci Notation = No Ambiguity

Convert to sci notation and leading zeros vanish. Trailing zeros in the coefficient are always significant. No guessing needed!

📍

The Decimal Dot Signal

That little dot is a signal: "I was careful — every digit shown is real." 500 ≠ 500. — they tell different stories about precision.
