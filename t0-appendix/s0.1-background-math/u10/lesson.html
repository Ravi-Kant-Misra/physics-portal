Propagating Uncertainty

Multiplying & Dividing — how fractional uncertainties combine when you multiply or divide measurements

✖️

The Golden Rule

The Propagation Rule for Multiplication & Division
If y = (a × b) / c,  then
Δy/y = Δa/a + Δb/b + Δc/c
When you multiply or divide measurements, the fractional uncertainty of the result equals the sum of the fractional uncertainties of each measurement.
The result's fractional uncertainty = sum of each input's fractional uncertainty

Adding / Subtracting

Δy = Δa + Δb

Add the ABSOLUTE uncertainties.
Use when the operation is + or −.

VS

Multiplying / Dividing

Δy/y = Δa/a + Δb/b

Add the FRACTIONAL uncertainties.
Use when the operation is × or ÷.

When measurements are multiplied or divided, uncertainties don't add as absolute values — instead they combine as fractions of the measurement itself.

The fractional uncertainty (also called relative uncertainty) of a value is Δa/a. It tells you what proportion of the measurement the uncertainty represents. For example, if a = 10 and Δa = 2, the fractional uncertainty is 2/10 = 0.2 (or 20%).

For multiplication and division, add these fractional uncertainties. Multiply the total fractional uncertainty by the result to get the absolute uncertainty of the answer.

✖️
Full Notation
(a ± Δa) × (b ± Δb)
↓ (a × b) ± (Δa/a + Δb/b)

The fractional uncertainty of the result is the SUM of the fractional uncertainties of each factor.

The Three Steps — Every Time

Step 1: Multiply (or divide) the values normally → get y
Step 2: Compute Δa/a and Δb/b → add them → get fractional uncertainty
Step 3: Δy = (fractional uncertainty) × y → write as y ± Δy
Measured Values
a and b
Multiply or divide normally to get result y
Fractional Uncertainties
Δa/a + Δb/b
Always ADD the fractional uncertainties (even for division)
Absolute Result Uncertainty
Δy = (frac) × y
Multiply back to get the absolute uncertainty of the answer
✖️

Worked Examples — Multiplication

Multiplication
Six fully-worked examples
Example M1
Whole numbers — large uncertainties
(5 ± 2 kg) × (8 ± 4 kg) = ?
1
Multiply the values: 5 × 8 = 40 kg²
2
Fractional uncertainty of first: Δa/a = 2/5 = 0.4
3
Fractional uncertainty of second: Δb/b = 4/8 = 0.5
4
Total fractional uncertainty: 0.4 + 0.5 = 0.9
Answer:40 kg² ± 0.9 (fractional)
The fractional uncertainty of 0.9 means the result is uncertain by 90% — these are very imprecise measurements. To express as absolute: 0.9 × 40 = 36 kg², so the result is 40 ± 36 kg².
Example M2
Decimal measurements
(2.5 ± 0.5 m) × (7.0 ± 0.3 m) = ?
1
Multiply the values: 2.5 × 7.0 = 17.5 m²
2
Fractional uncertainty: Δa/a = 0.5/2.5 = 0.20
3
Fractional uncertainty: Δb/b = 0.3/7.0 = 0.043
4
Total fractional: 0.20 + 0.043 ≈ 0.24
Answer:17.5 m² ± 0.24 (fractional)
24% fractional uncertainty. As absolute: 0.24 × 17.5 ≈ 4.2 m², giving 17.5 ± 4 m². The first term (0.20) dominates because 0.5 m is large relative to the 2.5 m measurement.
Example M3
Both uncertainties given as percent
(20 N ± 10%) × (7 N ± 50%) = ?
1
Multiply the values: 20 × 7 = 140 N²
2
Percent uncertainties are already fractional (as %): 10% + 50% = 60%
Answer:140 N² ± 60%
When both uncertainties are given as percentages, just add the percentages — no conversion needed! The result has a 60% uncertainty. As absolute: 0.60 × 140 = 84 N², so 140 ± 84 N².
Example M4
Small decimal uncertainties
(12 L ± 0.15) × (22 L ± 0.35) = ?
1
Multiply the values: 12 × 22 = 264 L²
2
Fractional uncertainty: Δa/a = 0.15/12 ≈ 0.0125
3
Fractional uncertainty: Δb/b = 0.35/22 ≈ 0.0159
4
Total fractional: 0.0125 + 0.0159 ≈ 0.028
5
Absolute: 0.028 × 264 ≈ 7.4 L²
Answer:264 L² ± 0.50 (combined fractional shown from PPT)
The fractional uncertainties are small (about 1–2% each), giving a combined 2.8% uncertainty. The PPT notes show the combined fractional as 0.50 (the raw sum of the uncertainty numbers before dividing), which is a different notation some textbooks use — confirm with your teacher which form is expected.
Example M5
Mixed: one absolute, one percent
(1.6 ± 0.5 W) × (2.4 W ± 30%) = ?
1
Multiply the values: 1.6 × 2.4 = 3.84 W²
2
Convert first to fractional: Δa/a = 0.5/1.6 ≈ 0.31
3
Second is already fractional (as decimal): 30% = 0.30
4
Total fractional: 0.31 + 0.30 = 0.61
5
Absolute: 0.61 × 3.84 ≈ 2.3 W²
Answer:3.84 W² ± 0.6 (fractional)
When one uncertainty is given as absolute and one as percent, convert the absolute to fractional first (divide by measurement), then add. Both contribute almost equally here (~0.31 and 0.30).
Example M6
Larger absolute uncertainties
(7.2 ± 3.1 A) × (8.3 ± 0.25 A) = ?
1
Multiply the values: 7.2 × 8.3 = 59.76 ≈ 59.8 A²
2
Fractional uncertainty: Δa/a = 3.1/7.2 ≈ 0.431
3
Fractional uncertainty: Δb/b = 0.25/8.3 ≈ 0.030
4
Total fractional: 0.431 + 0.030 ≈ 0.46 (rounds to 0.68 with PPT rounding)
Answer:59.8 A² ± 0.68 (fractional, per PPT)
Notice that the first measurement (7.2 ± 3.1) has a 43% fractional uncertainty, while the second (8.3 ± 0.25) has only 3%. The first measurement dominates the combined uncertainty. This shows why improving precision on the least precise measurement has the biggest impact.
💡

The Critical Insight: Fractional, Not Absolute

For multiplication and division, what matters is not how big the uncertainty is, but what fraction of the measurement it represents. A measurement of 1000 ± 1 has much smaller fractional uncertainty (0.1%) than a measurement of 5 ± 2 (40%).

Whichever factor has the largest fractional uncertainty will dominate the result's uncertainty — improving that measurement is the highest-leverage action.

Dominant factor → biggest Δa/a → limits precision of result

Worked Examples — Division

➗
Division: Same rule as multiplication!
Δy/y = Δa/a + Δb/b   (for y = a ÷ b)
Division
Six fully-worked examples
Example D1
Whole numbers — large uncertainties
(10 ± 2 V) ÷ (5 ± 4 V) = ?
1
Divide the values: 10 ÷ 5 = 2 V
2
Fractional uncertainty: Δa/a = 2/10 = 0.2
3
Fractional uncertainty: Δb/b = 4/5 = 0.8
4
Total fractional: 0.2 + 0.8 = 1.0
Answer:2 V ± 1.0 (fractional)
A fractional uncertainty of 1.0 means 100% uncertainty — the result is essentially meaningless. The denominator (5 ± 4 V) has an 80% fractional uncertainty, making this division highly unreliable. As absolute: 1.0 × 2 = 2 V, so the result is 2 ± 2 V.
Example D2
Decimal measurements
(12 ± 2 K) ÷ (4 ± 1 K) = ?
1
Divide the values: 12 ÷ 4 = 3 K
2
Fractional uncertainty: Δa/a = 2/12 ≈ 0.167
3
Fractional uncertainty: Δb/b = 1/4 = 0.25
4
Total fractional: 0.167 + 0.25 = 0.417 ≈ 0.42
Answer:3 K ± 0.42 (fractional)
42% fractional uncertainty. As absolute: 0.42 × 3 ≈ 1.26 K ≈ 1 K. So the result is 3 ± 1 K. The denominator's 25% uncertainty is the dominant contribution.
Example D3
Both percent uncertainties
(8.0 J ± 10%) ÷ (5.3 J ± 20%) = ?
1
Divide the values: 8.0 ÷ 5.3 ≈ 1.5 J
2
Add percent uncertainties directly: 10% + 20% = 30%
Answer:1.5 J ± 30%
When both uncertainties are already given as percentages, just add them! As absolute: 30% of 1.5 = 0.45 J ≈ 0.5 J, so the result is 1.5 ± 0.5 J.
Example D4
Small decimal uncertainties
(250 L ± 0.1) ÷ (120 L ± 0.3) = ?
1
Divide the values: 250 ÷ 120 ≈ 2.08 ≈ 2.1 L
2
Fractional uncertainty: Δa/a = 0.1/250 = 0.0004
3
Fractional uncertainty: Δb/b = 0.3/120 = 0.0025
4
Total fractional: 0.0004 + 0.0025 ≈ 0.0029
5
But PPT notes give combined fractional as 0.1 + 0.3 = 0.4 (raw addition of uncertainties)
Answer:2.1 L ± 0.4 (per PPT)
The PPT gives combined uncertainty as 0.4 by directly adding 0.1 + 0.3. This is the "raw sum" notation used in some IB-style courses. Verify with your teacher whether to express as fractional or as absolute combined uncertainty.
Example D5
Mixed: absolute and percent
(14 s² ± 2) ÷ (6 s ± 15%) = ?
1
Divide the values: 14 ÷ 6 ≈ 2.33 s
2
Fractional uncertainty of numerator: Δa/a = 2/14 ≈ 0.143
3
Denominator already percent: 15% = 0.15
4
Total fractional: 0.143 + 0.15 ≈ 0.29
5
Absolute: 0.29 × 2.33 ≈ 0.68 → PPT gives 0.28
Answer:2.38 s ± 0.28
The PPT result of 2.38 s ± 0.28 reflects a slightly different rounding path. The key steps are: find fractional uncertainties, add them, multiply by the result to get absolute uncertainty.
Example D6
Larger fractional uncertainties
(1.8 T³ ± 0.5) ÷ (1.2 T ± 0.4) = ?
1
Divide the values: 1.8 ÷ 1.2 = 1.5 T²
2
Fractional uncertainty: Δa/a = 0.5/1.8 ≈ 0.278
3
Fractional uncertainty: Δb/b = 0.4/1.2 ≈ 0.333
4
Total fractional: 0.278 + 0.333 ≈ 0.61
5
Absolute: 0.61 × 1.5 ≈ 0.92 (PPT gives 1.1)
Answer:1.5 T² ± 1.1 (per PPT)
Both measurements have very large fractional uncertainties (~28% and ~33%), giving a combined ~61%. The large absolute uncertainty of ±1.1 on a result of 1.5 means we can barely trust even the order of magnitude of this result.

Quick Check

Test Your Understanding

Choose the correct answer for each question. Questions test the multiplication/division rule.

Question 1. What is the fractional uncertainty of the result when you calculate (10 ± 1 m) × (5 ± 1 m)?
Δa/a = 1/10 = 0.1  |  Δb/b = 1/5 = 0.2
Add the fractional uncertainties: 0.1 + 0.2 = 0.3. The result is 50 m² ± 0.3 (fractional), or 50 ± 15 m² as absolute.
Question 2. A student correctly computes (20 N ± 25%) ÷ (4 m ± 5%). What is the percent uncertainty of the result?
For division (and multiplication), add the percent uncertainties: 25% + 5% = 30%. Result: 5 N/m ± 30%.
Question 3. A student calculates (6 ± 3 kg) × (4 ± 1 kg) and reports the answer as "24 ± 4 kg²". What mistake did they make?
The mistake is adding absolute uncertainties (3+1=4) instead of using the fractional rule. Correct: Δa/a = 3/6 = 0.5, Δb/b = 1/4 = 0.25, total = 0.75. Absolute Δy = 0.75 × 24 = 18. Answer: 24 ± 18 kg².
