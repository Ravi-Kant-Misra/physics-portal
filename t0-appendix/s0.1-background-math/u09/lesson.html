Propagating Uncertainty

Addition & Subtraction — how errors combine when you add or subtract measurements

📐

The Golden Rule

The Propagation Rule for Addition & Subtraction
If y = a ± b, then Δy = Δa + Δb
When you add or subtract two measurements, the uncertainty of the result equals the sum of the absolute uncertainties of each measurement.
⚠️ The uncertainties always ADD — even when you subtract the values!

Every real measurement carries uncertainty. When you combine measurements through arithmetic, those uncertainties don't just disappear — they propagate (carry through) into your result.

For addition and subtraction, the rule is beautifully simple: add the absolute uncertainties together. This is because uncertainties represent a range of possible values, and the worst-case scenario is when both uncertainties push the result in the same direction.

The result uncertainty is always the sum of the individual uncertainties — this means your result can never be more precise than your least precise measurement.

➕
Full Notation
(a ± Δa) ± (b ± Δb)
↓ (a ± b) ± (Δa + Δb)

The measured values combine normally. The uncertainties always add.

⚠️ Why Do Uncertainties Add Even When We Subtract?

1
Each measurement has a range: a ± Δa could be as large as a + Δa or as small as a − Δa.
2
The worst case occurs when a is at its maximum AND b is at its maximum — they both push the uncertainty in the same direction at the same time.
3
Even for subtraction (y = a − b), the worst case for the uncertainty is still when both Δa and Δb act together, giving Δy = Δa + Δb.
4
Think of it this way: if you subtract two lengths and each can be off by 1 cm in any direction, the result could be off by up to 2 cm — not 0 cm!

Breaking Down the Notation

(a ± Δa) + (b ± Δb) = (a + b) ± (Δa + Δb)
Measured values
a and b
Combine normally: add or subtract as required
Uncertainties
Δa and Δb
Always ADD together, regardless of + or −
Result
Δy = Δa + Δb
Round uncertainty to 1 sig fig, match decimal places

Worked Examples — Addition

Addition
Six fully-worked examples
Example 1
Simple whole numbers
(5 ± 2 kg) + (8 ± 4 kg) = ?
1
Add the values: 5 + 8 = 13 kg
2
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 2 + 4 = 6 kg
Answer:13 ± 6 kg
Both measurements have large uncertainties. The result's uncertainty (6 kg) is the direct sum of both — there is no "cancellation" of uncertainty.
Example 2
Decimal measurements
(2.5 ± 0.5 m) + (7.0 ± 0.3 m) = ?
1
Add the values: 2.5 + 7.0 = 9.5 m
2
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 0.5 + 0.3 = 0.8 m
Answer:9.5 ± 0.8 m
The uncertainty (0.8 m) already has 1 sig fig and matches the decimal places of the measurement (tenths). No further rounding needed.
Example 3
Converting percent uncertainty first
(20 N ± 10%) + (7 N ± 50%) = ?
1
Convert 10% of 20 N to absolute: 20 × 0.10 = 2 N, so first measurement is 20 ± 2 N
2
Convert 50% of 7 N to absolute: 7 × 0.50 = 3.5 N, so second measurement is 7 ± 3.5 N
3
Add the values: 20 + 7 = 27 N
4
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 2 + 3.5 = 5.5 N
Answer:27 ± 5.5 N
Always convert percent uncertainty to absolute before applying the addition rule. Δy = 5.5 N has 2 sig figs — rounding to 1 sig fig would give 27 ± 6 N, which is also acceptable.
Example 4
Small decimal uncertainties
(12 L ± 0.15) + (22 L ± 0.35) = ?
1
Add the values: 12 + 22 = 34 L
2
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 0.15 + 0.35 = 0.50 L
Answer:34 L ± 0.50
The uncertainty 0.50 has 2 significant figures. When expressed to 1 sig fig it would be 0.5, and the measurement is written to match: 34.0 ± 0.5 L. Both 34 ± 0.50 and 34.0 ± 0.5 are correct here.
Example 5
One percent, one absolute
(1.6 ± 0.5 W) + (2.4 W ± 30%) = ?
1
Convert 30% of 2.4 W: 2.4 × 0.30 = 0.72 W, so second measurement is 2.4 ± 0.72 W
2
Add the values: 1.6 + 2.4 = 4.0 W
3
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 0.5 + 0.72 = 1.22 W
4
Round uncertainty to 1 sig fig: 1.22 → 1.2 W 1 s.f. on Δ
Answer:4.0 ± 1.2 W
One uncertainty was in percent, one in absolute — convert to absolute first, then add. Round the final uncertainty to 1 significant figure, then match the measurement's decimal places.
Example 6
Large absolute uncertainty — rounding carefully
(7.2 ± 3.1 A) + (8.3 ± 0.25 A) = ?
1
Add the values: 7.2 + 8.3 = 15.5 A
2
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 3.1 + 0.25 = 3.35 A
3
Round uncertainty to 1 sig fig: 3.35 → 3 A. Match measurement to units: 15.5 → 16 A? Actually round to tenths: 3.35 ≈ 3.4 A (keeping 1 s.f. is 3; keeping 2 s.f. is 3.4) note
Answer:15.5 ± 3.4 A(or 15.5 ± 3 A if rounding Δ to 1 s.f.)
Both answers are defensible. Rounding 3.35 to 1 sig fig gives 3 A (measurement then rounds to 16 A). Keeping 2 sig figs gives 3.4 A (measurement stays 15.5 A). In IB Physics, 1 significant figure on the uncertainty is standard, but 2 is acceptable when the leading digit is 3 or less.

Worked Examples — Subtraction

⚡
Remember: When subtracting measurements, the values subtract but the uncertainties STILL ADD!
y = a − b → Δy = Δa + Δb (NOT Δa − Δb)
Example 7
Uncertainty larger than result — still valid!
(10 ± 2 V) − (5 ± 4 V) = ?
1
Subtract the values: 10 − 5 = 5 V
2
ADD the uncertainties: Δy = 2 + 4 = 6 V add, not subtract!
Answer:5 ± 6 V
The uncertainty (±6 V) is larger than the result (5 V)! This is perfectly valid — it simply means the measurement is not very meaningful. A result of 5 ± 6 V includes zero, so we cannot even be certain the true value is positive.
Example 8
Temperature difference
(12 ± 2 K) − (4 ± 1 K) = ?
1
Subtract the values: 12 − 4 = 8 K
2
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 2 + 1 = 3 K
Answer:8 ± 3 K
This could represent a temperature change in an experiment. Even though we subtracted, the uncertainty grew — we added 2 K and 1 K to get 3 K total uncertainty.
Example 9
Decimal — rounding to 1 sig fig on uncertainty
(8.0 ± 0.18) − (5.3 ± 0.25) = ?
1
Subtract the values: 8.0 − 5.3 = 2.7
2
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 0.18 + 0.25 = 0.43
3
Round uncertainty to 1 sig fig: 0.43 → 0.4. Measurement already to tenths. match d.p.
Answer:2.7 ± 0.4
Rounding the uncertainty 0.43 to 1 significant figure gives 0.4. The measurement 2.7 already has one decimal place, matching the rounded uncertainty perfectly.
Example 10
Volume difference — precise instruments
(250 L ± 0.1) − (120 L ± 0.3) = ?
1
Subtract the values: 250 − 120 = 130 L
2
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 0.1 + 0.3 = 0.4 L
Answer:130 ± 0.4 L
The very small uncertainty (±0.4 L compared to 130 L) indicates precise instruments were used. The result should be written as 130.0 ± 0.4 L to show the measurement's precision to tenths.
Example 11
Converting percent uncertainty
(14 ± 2 s) − (6 s ± 15%) = ?
1
Convert 15% of 6 s to absolute: 6 × 0.15 = 0.9 s, so second measurement is 6 ± 0.9 s
2
Subtract the values: 14 − 6 = 8 s
3
Add the uncertainties: Δy = 2 + 0.9 = 2.9 s
4
Round to 1 sig fig: 2.9 → 3 s. Match measurement: 8 s is already to units. round Δ
Answer:8 ± 3 s
Convert percent to absolute first, then apply the rule. Rounding 2.9 to 1 sig fig gives 3, so the result is 8 ± 3 s.
Example 12
Unitless fractional notation
(1.8 ± 0.5 T) − (1.2 T ± 0.4) = ?
1
Note: the second uncertainty 0.4 has no unit — this is a fractional uncertainty. Convert to absolute: 1.2 × 0.4 = 0.48 ≈ 0.5 T
2
Subtract the values: 1.8 − 1.2 = 0.6 T
3
Add the absolute uncertainties: Δy = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1.0 T
Answer:0.6 ± 1.0 T
When the uncertainty is written without a unit after it (e.g., "±0.4" not "±0.4 T"), it indicates a fractional (dimensionless) uncertainty. Convert to absolute before combining. The result 0.6 ± 1.0 T again shows the uncertainty exceeds the measurement — poor precision!
💡

The Key Insight: Uncertainties Always Add

Whether you are adding or subtracting your measurements, the rule for combining uncertainties is always the same: add the absolute uncertainties. There is no such thing as "subtracting uncertainty."

This is because uncertainty represents a range of possible values — and the worst-case scenario is always when both uncertainties act in the same direction, regardless of whether you're adding or subtracting the central values.

This means: subtraction never reduces uncertainty. In fact, subtracting two nearly-equal measurements is dangerous — the relative uncertainty of the result can become enormous.

Δy = Δa + Δb always (for + and −)
