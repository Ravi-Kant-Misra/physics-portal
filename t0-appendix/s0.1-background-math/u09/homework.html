Reference — The Rule

For addition (y = a + b):
Δy = Δa + Δb
For subtraction (y = a − b):
Δy = Δa + Δb (same — always add!)
Percent → Absolute:
Δa_abs = (% / 100) × a
Fractional → Absolute:
Δa_abs = frac × a
Key reminder: Uncertainties ALWAYS add, whether you are adding or subtracting the measured values. Round the final uncertainty to 1 sig fig, then match the measurement's decimal places.

Answer Key

Part A — Harder Calculations

Problem 1
(35±5% kg) + (15±20% kg)
50 ± 5 kg
5% of 35=1.75; 20% of 15=3. Values: 35+15=50. Δy=1.75+3=4.75→5 kg (1 s.f.).
Problem 2
(120±5% V) − (80±15% V)
40 ± 18 V
5% of 120=6 V; 15% of 80=12 V. Values: 120−80=40 V. Δy=6+12=18 V.
Problem 3
(4.75±0.12 s) + (2.38±0.09 s)
7.13 ± 0.21 s → 7.1 ± 0.2 s
Values: 4.75+2.38=7.13 s. Δy=0.12+0.09=0.21→0.2 s (1 s.f.). Match to tenths: 7.1 ± 0.2 s.
Problem 4
(550±25 N) − (320±40 N)
230 ± 65 N → 230 ± 60 N
Values: 550−320=230 N. Δy=25+40=65→60 N (1 s.f.). Match: 230 ± 60 N.
Problem 5
(8±1) + (5±2) + (3±1) m
16 ± 4 m
Values: 8+5+3=16 m. Δy=1+2+1=4 m. The rule extends to any number of measurements.
Problem 6
(2.4±0.25 frac T) − (1.0±0.5 T)
1.4 ± 1.1 T
Frac: 0.25×2.4=0.6 T. Values: 2.4−1.0=1.4 T. Δy=0.6+0.5=1.1 T.

Part B — True or False

Statement 7
FALSE
You must ALWAYS add uncertainties. Subtracting them would underestimate the error range.
Statement 8
TRUE
Δy = Δa + Δb applies to both operations — the sign of the operation on the values does not affect how uncertainties combine.
Statement 9
FALSE
An uncertainty larger than the central value is perfectly valid — it simply means the measurement is very imprecise, and the true value could include zero or even be negative.
Statement 10
TRUE
The rule Δy = Δa + Δb only works with absolute uncertainties. Convert percent or fractional forms first.
Statement 11
FALSE
Subtracting nearly equal values is dangerous — the absolute uncertainty stays large while the result becomes tiny, causing the relative uncertainty to become enormous. This is called "loss of significance."
Statement 12
TRUE
Adding three measurements: Δy = 2 + 2 + 2 = 6 cm. The rule extends — add all individual uncertainties.

Part C — Extended Analysis

Question 13:
(a) ΔT = 85.0 − 82.0 = 3.0°C. Uncertainty: 1.5 + 1.5 = 3.0°C. So ΔT = 3.0 ± 3.0°C.
(b) The claim is not valid. The uncertainty equals the result — the temperature change could be anywhere from 0°C to 6.0°C. We cannot conclude the liquid cooled at all since zero is within the uncertainty range. The physicist would need a more precise thermometer.
Question 14:
(a) Student A: 12.3 ± 0.1 cm; Student B: 12.6 ± 0.1 cm.
(b) Difference: 12.6 − 12.3 = 0.3 cm. Uncertainty: 0.1 + 0.1 = 0.2 cm. So difference = 0.3 ± 0.2 cm.
(c) The measurements are consistent. "Consistent" means the uncertainty ranges overlap: A's range is 12.2–12.4 cm; B's range is 12.5–12.7 cm. The ranges just barely touch (they share the boundary at 12.4–12.5). The difference of 0.3 cm could plausibly be due to measurement uncertainty alone — the students could be measuring the same length. Note: the difference (0.3 cm) is only marginally larger than the uncertainty (0.2 cm), so we cannot firmly say they measured different values.
Question 15:
(a) Total time: 62 + 58 = 120 s. Uncertainty: 3 + 3 = 6 s. Total = 120 ± 6 s.
(b) Difference: 62 − 58 = 4 s. Uncertainty: 3 + 3 = 6 s. Difference = 4 ± 6 s.
(c) The coach's claim is not supported. The uncertainty on the difference (±6 s) is larger than the difference itself (4 s). The true difference could be anywhere from −2 s to +10 s, which includes zero (no difference) and even negative values (Lap 1 faster!). We cannot conclude Lap 2 was definitively faster.

Part D — Bonus Challenge

Question 16:
(a) 5% of 38 = 1.9 cm, so Section 2 = 38 ± 1.9 cm (≈ 38 ± 2 cm).
(b) Total = 42 + 38 + 20 = 100 cm. Uncertainty: 2 + 1.9 + 1 = 4.9 ≈ 5 cm. Total = 100 ± 5 cm.
(c) After cutting Section 3 (20 ± 1 cm) from the total (100 ± 5 cm): Remaining = 100 − 20 = 80 cm. Uncertainty: 5 + 1 = 6 cm. Result = 80 ± 6 cm.
(d) Percent uncertainty = (6/80) × 100% = 7.5%. So 80 ± 7.5% cm.
