📌 Rules Reminder

1
Analogue instruments: Uncertainty = (½ × smallest increment) × number of measurements taken
2
Digital instruments: Uncertainty = ±1 digit of the last significant figure displayed
!
Always report the LARGER of the instrument limit (Option 1) and your justified experimental estimate (Option 2)

Answer Key

Part A & B — Instrument Uncertainties & Full Measurements

Problem 1 — Metric Ruler (1 mm increments, 2 measurements)
± 0.1 cm  →  14.2 ± 0.1 cm
½ × 0.1 cm = 0.05 cm × 2 = 0.1 cm
Problem 2 — Analogue Thermometer (0.5 °C, 1 measurement)
± 0.25 °C  →  36.5 ± 0.25 °C
½ × 0.5 °C = 0.25 °C × 1 = 0.25 °C
Problem 3 — Spring Balance (0.2 N, 1 measurement)
± 0.1 N  →  4.8 ± 0.1 N
½ × 0.2 N = 0.1 N × 1 = 0.1 N
Problem 4 — Analogue Voltmeter (0.5 V, 2 measurements)
± 0.5 V  →  6.5 ± 0.5 V
½ × 0.5 V = 0.25 V × 2 = 0.5 V
Problem 5 — Digital Balance (reads 45.67 g)
± 0.01 g  →  45.67 ± 0.01 g
Last digit in hundredths place → ±0.01 g
Problem 6 — Digital Stopwatch (reads 12.34 s)
± 0.01 s  →  12.34 ± 0.01 s
Last digit in hundredths place → ±0.01 s

Part C — Choosing the Larger Uncertainty

Problem 7: Report ±0.4 cm (Option 2). The wobbling beaker introduces more uncertainty than the ruler's resolution. The experimenter's justified estimate is larger and must be reported.
Problem 8: Report ±0.01 A. Both options give the same value. Either can be reported — the experimenter confirmed the procedure introduced no additional uncertainty beyond the instrument limit.
Problem 9: Report ±0.5 °F (Option 2). The patient's movement creates genuine variability that exceeds the digital instrument's resolution. The larger, justified estimate is more honest.

Part D — Short Answer Guidance

Problem 10: Sample answer — The instrument limit only accounts for the physical resolution of the device. Real experiments also have procedural limitations (parallax, vibration, reaction time, technique variation) that can make the actual uncertainty much larger than the instrument alone suggests. Reporting only the instrument limit could give a false sense of precision.
Problem 11: Sample answer — "The measurement uncertainty for the spring length is ±0.1 cm because the ruler had a smallest increment of 0.1 cm, giving an instrument limit of 0.05 cm per reading; with two measurements (start and end), the instrument uncertainty is 0.1 cm, which is larger than my estimated procedural uncertainty of ±0.05 cm due to the spring being nearly stationary."
Problem 12: The student fails to report any uncertainty, which makes the measurement appear perfectly precise. They should write 250.0 ± 0.1 g, since the last digit on the digital display is in the tenths place, giving an uncertainty of ±0.1 g.
