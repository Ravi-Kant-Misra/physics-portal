Measurement Uncertainty

Every measuring device is imperfect — learn how to quantify exactly how imperfect it is

🤔

What Is Measurement Uncertainty?

Whenever you measure something — with a ruler, a thermometer, a stopwatch, or a digital scale — you are using a device that has physical limits. No instrument is perfectly precise. Measurement uncertainty is the result of this imprecision.

All measuring devices have some degree of imprecision. That means every measurement you record must be accompanied by an uncertainty value to tell the reader how confident you are in that reading.

Think of it this way: if a ruler's smallest marking is 1 mm, you simply cannot know whether the true length is 23.4 mm or 23.6 mm — both look the same. The uncertainty captures this gap between what you can see and what is truly there.

We write a measurement with its uncertainty using the ± notation:

measurement  ±  uncertainty

Example:   23.4 ± 0.5 cm

⚖️

The Core Rule — Always Take the Larger

The Golden Rule of Measurement Uncertainty

Measurement uncertainty is always the LARGER of:
1
The limit of the instrument (half the smallest increment) multiplied by the number of measurements taken
↕ whichever is BIGGER
2
A justified estimation of the limit of the measurement procedure as done by the experimenter

Why two options? Option 1 gives the theoretical minimum from the instrument's physical markings. Option 2 accounts for real-world issues: a wobbly object, parallax when reading a scale, reaction time, inconsistent technique. If your experimental setup makes things messier than the instrument's resolution, you must use the larger (more honest) estimate.

🔍

Reading the Smallest Increment

How to Identify the Smallest Increment on a Scale

The smallest increment is the gap between the two closest markings on an analogue scale. Here is how it looks on three common instruments:

Ruler
Smallest increment = 0.5 cm

Numbers are labeled every 1 cm, with a tick halfway between. The gap between adjacent ticks is 0.5 cm — that is your smallest increment.

Voltmeter with needle
Smallest increment = 0.2 V

Each space between labeled marks is divided into 5 minor divisions, making each minor division 0.2 V. The needle here reads approximately 3.3 V.

Digital scale
Smallest displayed unit = 0.1 g

Digital instruments show a fixed number of decimal places. The uncertainty is at least ±1 of that last digit.

📏

Analogue (Physical) Instruments

📏
Analogue Instruments
Ruler, thermometer, voltmeter with needle, spring balance, etc.
  • 1
    Identify the smallest increment — the gap between the two closest markings on the scale
  • 2
    Calculate the instrument limit: half the smallest increment ( = smallest increment ÷ 2 )
  • 3
    Count the number of measurements needed to get the final value
  • 4
    Multiply: uncertainty = instrument limit × number of measurements
Formula
Uncertainty = (½ × smallest increment) × nmeasurements
Example 1 — Ruler
Instrument
Metric ruler
Smallest increment
0.5 cm
1
Measuring a pencil requires reading the start position and the end position → 2 measurements
2
Instrument limit = ½ × 0.5 cm = 0.25 cm
3
Uncertainty = 0.25 cm × 2 = 0.5 cm
Length = 14.5 ± 0.5 cm
Example 2 — Voltmeter with Needle
Instrument
Analogue voltmeter
Smallest increment
0.2 V
1
Measuring a voltage rise: read the starting position and the ending position of the needle → 2 measurements
2
Instrument limit = ½ × 0.2 V = 0.1 V
3
Uncertainty = 0.1 V × 2 = 0.2 V
Voltage = 3.3 ± 0.2 V
💻

Digital Instruments

🔢
Digital Instruments
Digital scale, digital multimeter, digital thermometer, etc.
  • 1
    Read the value shown on the display
  • 2
    The uncertainty is at least ± 1 digit of the last (rightmost) significant figure
Rule for Digital Instruments
Uncertainty = ± 1 digit of last significant figure
Example — Digital Scale
Display reads
136.4 g
Last digit place
tenths (0.1 g)
1
The last significant figure is in the tenths column → 0.1 g precision
2
Uncertainty = ±1 × 0.1 g = ± 0.1 g
Mass = 136.4 ± 0.1 g

Lab Report — How to Write About Uncertainty

📝
Writing Tip for the IV / DV Section
Use this exact phrasing (or close to it) when describing measurement uncertainty in your lab report

"Measurement uncertainty is always the larger of:

  1. If using a physical measuring tool like a metre stick: the limit of the instrument (half the smallest increment) multiplied by the number of measurements taken. If using a digital reading: the uncertainty will be at least ±1 digit of the last significant figure of the reading.
  2. A justified estimation of the limit of the measurement procedure as done by the experimenter."
Remember: Whenever you state an uncertainty, you must justify your choice. Explain why you chose Option 1 or Option 2, and why one was larger than the other in your specific experiment.
