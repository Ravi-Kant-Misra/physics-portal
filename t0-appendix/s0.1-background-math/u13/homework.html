Answer Key

Part A — Calculations

Problem 1 — Graduated Cylinder (2 mL increments, 1 reading)
Uncertainty = ½ × 2 mL × 1 = 1 mL  →  Volume = 46 ± 1 mL
Instrument limit per reading = ½ × 2 = 1 mL. Only one reading (one meniscus position), so multiply by 1.
Problem 2 — Analogue Ammeter (0.1 A increments, 2 measurements)
Uncertainty = ½ × 0.1 A × 2 = 0.1 A  →  Current = 1.4 ± 0.1 A
Instrument limit = ½ × 0.1 = 0.05 A per reading. Two readings → 0.05 × 2 = 0.1 A.
Problem 3 — Digital Multimeter (5.026 V)
Uncertainty = ±0.001 V  →  Voltage = 5.026 ± 0.001 V
Last digit is in the thousandths place (0.001 V), so uncertainty = ±0.001 V.
Problem 4 — Metre Rule (0.1 cm increments, 2 measurements)
Uncertainty = ½ × 0.1 cm × 2 = 0.1 cm  →  Wavelength = 32.4 ± 0.1 cm
Instrument limit = 0.05 cm per reading. Two node positions → 0.05 × 2 = 0.1 cm.
Problem 5 — Micrometer (3.45 mm)
Uncertainty = ±0.01 mm  →  Diameter = 3.45 ± 0.01 mm
Digital-style readout: last digit in hundredths place (0.01 mm) → uncertainty = ±0.01 mm.

Part B — Real-World Scenarios

Problem 6 — Stopwatch (reaction time)
a) ±0.01 s    b) 2 × ±0.2 s = ±0.4 s    c) Report ±0.4 s
The instrument uncertainty (0.01 s) is much smaller than the human reaction-time uncertainty (0.4 s from two button presses). The larger value (±0.4 s) must be reported. This is a key real-world application: a high-precision digital instrument does not eliminate human error in the procedure.
Problem 7 — Blood Pressure (2 mmHg increments, 2 readings)
Instrument uncertainty = ½ × 2 × 2 = 2 mmHg. Patient fidgeting = ±5 mmHg → Report ±5 mmHg.
Blood pressure = (120 ± 5) / (80 ± 5) mmHg
The experimental estimate (±5 mmHg) dominates. Both the systolic and diastolic readings carry the same uncertainty of ±5 mmHg.
Problem 8 — Rebound Ball Photography
a) Drop height: ½ × 0.1 × 2 = 0.1 cm    b) Ruler instrument only: 0.1 cm    c) Photo reading adds ±1.0 cm → Report ±1.0 cm for rebound height
The photograph introduces parallax and scaling errors that dominate over the ruler's instrument uncertainty. The drop height (measured directly) remains ±0.1 cm, but the rebound height (read from a photo) must use the larger experimental estimate of ±1.0 cm.

Part C — True or False

Statement 1
TRUE
8.00 s shows hundredths precision; ±0.01 s is correct for a digital instrument.
Statement 2
FALSE
The instrument uncertainty per measurement is HALF the smallest increment: ½ × 0.5 cm = 0.25 cm. (The total after multiplying by number of measurements may equal 0.5 cm if two readings are taken, but per measurement it is 0.25 cm.)
Statement 3
TRUE
Always report the larger value. ±0.3 cm > ±0.1 cm, so ±0.3 cm is correct.
Statement 4
FALSE
All measuring devices — old or new, cheap or expensive — have some imprecision. Measurement uncertainty is a fundamental feature of measurement, not a fault of the equipment.
Statement 5
FALSE
Measuring a table with a ruler involves 2 readings: where the ruler starts (0 end) and where the table ends. Both positions must be read on the scale → n = 2 measurements.
Statement 6
TRUE
Even a zero reading carries uncertainty. The display resolves to 0.01 g, so ±0.01 g applies regardless of the value shown.

Part D — Extended Writing (Model Answers)

Problem 9 — Accuracy vs. Measurement Uncertainty
The student is confusing accuracy (how close a measurement is to the true value) with measurement uncertainty (the range of values within which the true measurement likely lies). Even a highly accurate digital balance has a finite resolution — its last digit represents a limit of ±1 digit that cannot be escaped regardless of the instrument's quality. Furthermore, measurement uncertainty also includes the experimental procedure: table vibrations, balance drift, or placing items off-centre all contribute additional uncertainty beyond the instrument. Every measurement must report an uncertainty; there is no such thing as a perfectly certain measurement in physics.
Problem 10 — Spring Extension Lab
(a) The instrument uncertainty = ½ × 0.1 cm × 2 = 0.1 cm (two readings: natural length and extended length). (b) Experimental factors that increase uncertainty: the spring oscillates slightly after adding mass, making it hard to read a stable position; parallax error when reading the ruler at an angle; the spring not hanging perfectly vertically. (c) If any of these factors justify an estimate larger than 0.1 cm (e.g., ±0.3 cm due to oscillation), then ±0.3 cm must be reported and justified because we always use the larger, more honest value.
Problem 11 — Two Students, Same Scale
No — the two students will report different uncertainties despite using the same instrument. Student A's procedure is stable: the instrument uncertainty of ±0.1 g dominates, and they report 0.1 g. Student B's moving object causes the display to fluctuate by ±0.5 g, which is a justified experimental estimate larger than the instrument limit. By the two-option rule, Student B must report ±0.5 g. This demonstrates that measurement uncertainty describes the experiment, not just the device — the same tool can yield different uncertainties depending on how well the measurement procedure is executed.
