IB Physics · Unit 19

Recording the Angle
of a Vector

How to precisely label a vector's direction anywhere in the full 360° circle using compass notation

🧭

Why Do We Need a Notation?

We measure angles as values between 0° and 90°. But a vector can point in any direction — a full 360° circle. So the angle alone doesn't tell us enough.

For example, "45°" could mean northeast, northwest, southeast, or southwest. We need a system that specifies which part of the circle the vector lives in.

Convention: we always measure the angle between the vector and the horizontal axis, then describe the direction using compass points (N, S, E, W).

The format is: "X degrees [North/South] of [East/West]"
The second compass direction names the horizontal reference axis. The first names the direction you tilt away from it.

30° East of North

Start at North (vertical), rotate 30° toward East.

30° East of North

30° North of East

Start at East (horizontal), rotate 30° toward North.

30° North of East

These two vectors are NOT the same!

"30° East of North" and "30° North of East" point in different directions. The angle (30°) is the same but the reference axis is different — one is 30° from vertical, the other is 30° from horizontal. Together they sum to 90°.

📐

How to Read the Notation

The Formula

"[angle]° [Direction A] of [Direction B]"
Direction B (second word)

The reference axis. This is where you start. Your angle is measured FROM this direction.

Direction A (first word)

The tilt direction. You rotate away from Direction B and toward this compass direction.

The Angle

Always between 0° and 90°. It's the size of the rotation from the reference axis.

Quick Check

"North of East" → horizontal reference, tilted up. "East of North" → vertical reference, tilted right.

Memory trick: read it like a city address — "30° East of North" means you are East relative to North. So North is your starting point.

The Drawing Convention

Normal / Standard

Angle Arc from the Horizontal

In IB Physics, we almost always draw the angle arc between the vector and the horizontal axis. This gives us the "_ of East/West" forms:

  • North of East
  • North of West
  • South of East
  • South of West
Rarely Used

Angle Arc from the Vertical

Sometimes you'll see the angle drawn from the vertical axis. This gives the "_ of North/South" forms:

  • East of North
  • East of South
  • West of North
  • West of South

Same vector, two valid labels: The green vector in both diagrams above is the same direction — 40° North of East (standard) = 50° East of North (rare form). Both are correct; IB Physics almost always uses the horizontal-reference form.
