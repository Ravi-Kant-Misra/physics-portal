Proportional Relationships

Linear, Quadratic, Inverse, and Inverse Square — the four shapes of physics equations

∝

The Proportionality Symbol ∝

The symbol ∝ means "is proportional to." When we write y ∝ x, we mean that y and x are related by a constant multiplier — doubling x doubles y, tripling x triples y, and so on.

y ∝ x  ⟺  y = cx

"y is proportional to x" means y equals some constant c multiplied by x. The constant c doesn't change — it's a fixed property of the system (like mass, or gravitational constant).

Proportionality is also symmetric: if y ∝ x then x ∝ y. But because y = cx, we get x = (1/c)y — the constant flips.

y ∝ x⟷x ∝ y
y = cx⟷x = (1/c)y

If y is proportional to x, x is proportional to y — but with a reciprocal constant.

📈

The Four Proportional Relationships

Linear   y ∝ x
y ∝ x
y = cx
  • Straight line through origin
  • Double x → double y
  • y/x = constant
Quadratic   y ∝ x²
y ∝ x²
y = cx²
  • Parabola through origin
  • Double x → quadruple y
  • y/x² = constant
Inverse   y ∝ 1/x
y ∝ 1/x
y = c/x   (= c·x⁻¹)
  • Hyperbola — never touches axes
  • Double x → halve y
  • xy = constant
Inverse Square   y ∝ 1/x²
y ∝ 1/x²
y = c/x²   (= c·x⁻²)
  • Steeper drop than inverse
  • Double x → quarter y
  • yx² = constant
🔍

How to Identify the Relationship from an Equation

The Method — Hold all other variables constant

1
Identify the two variables you are comparing (y and x)
2
Hold all other variables constant — treat them as part of the constant c
3
Rearrange so the equation looks like one of: y = cx, y = cx², y = c/x, or y = c/x²
4
If both variables appear on the same side, rearrange so one variable is on each side
5
Match the form to one of the four relationships and identify the graph shape
Example 1
F = ma  — Relationship between Force and Acceleration?
F = ma  (hold mass m constant)

Steps

1
Variables: F and a
2
Hold m constant → m = c
3
Equation becomes: F = c·a
4
This matches y = cx ✓

Conclusion

LINEAR relationship
  • F ∝ a
  • Force is proportional to acceleration
  • F vs. a graph is a straight line
Example 2
F = ma  — Relationship between Mass and Acceleration?
F = ma  (hold force F constant)

Steps

1
Variables: m and a
2
Hold F constant → F = c
3
Rearrange: m = F/a = c/a = c·a⁻¹
4
Matches y = c/x ✓

Conclusion

INVERSE relationship
  • m ∝ 1/a
  • Mass is proportional to 1/acceleration
  • m vs. a graph is a hyperbola
Example 3
g = Gm/r²  — Relationship between gravitational field g and mass m?
g = Gm/r²  (hold r constant)

Steps

1
Variables: g and m
2
Hold G and r constant → G/r² = c
3
Equation: g = c·m
4
Matches y = cx ✓

Conclusion

LINEAR relationship
  • g ∝ m
  • Gravitational field ∝ mass
  • g vs. m graph is a straight line
Example 4
g = Gm/r²  — Relationship between mass m and distance r?
g = Gm/r²  (hold g and G constant)

Steps

1
Variables: m and r
2
Hold g and G constant: g/G = c
3
Rearrange: c·r² = m → m = c·r²
4
Matches y = cx² ✓

Conclusion

QUADRATIC relationship
  • m ∝ r²
  • Mass is proportional to distance squared
  • m vs. r graph is a parabola
📊

Reading Relationships from Graphs

You can also identify proportional relationships by looking at the shape of a graph — assuming all other variables are held constant.

Linear — Momentum vs. Velocity

p ∝ v — straight line through origin. Momentum is proportional to velocity.

Quadratic — Kinetic Energy vs. Velocity

Eₖ ∝ v² — parabola. Kinetic energy is quadratically proportional to velocity.

Inverse — Resistance vs. Current

R ∝ 1/I — hyperbola. Resistance is inversely proportional to current.

Inverse Square — Electric Field vs. Distance

E ∝ 1/d² — inverse square curve, drops faster than plain inverse.
