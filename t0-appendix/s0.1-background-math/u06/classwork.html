Answer Key

Part A — Identify the Relationship

Q1: p = mv, p vs v
Linear (p ∝ v)
m = constant c; p = c·v → straight line
Q2: Eₖ = ½mv², Eₖ vs v
Quadratic (Eₖ ∝ v²)
½m = c; Eₖ = c·v² → parabola
Q3: V = IR, V vs I
Linear (V ∝ I)
R = constant c; V = c·I → straight line
Q4: V = IR, R vs I
Inverse (R ∝ 1/I)
R = V/I = c/I → hyperbola
Q5: g = Gm/r², g vs r
Inverse Square (g ∝ 1/r²)
Hold Gm = c; g = c/r² → steep inverse-square curve

Part B — Graph to Relationship

Q6: Eₖ vs v² is linear
Eₖ ∝ v² (Quadratic vs v)
Linear on Eₖ vs v² plot → quadratic on Eₖ vs v plot
Q7: xy = const, hyperbola
Inverse (y ∝ 1/x, y = c/x)
Hyperbola shape; product is constant

Part C — Predictions

Q8: v doubles in Eₖ = ½mv²
Eₖ becomes 4× larger
Eₖ ∝ v²; (2v)² = 4v² → Eₖ quadruples
Q9: r doubles in g = Gm/r²
g becomes ¼ as large
g ∝ 1/r²; (2r)² = 4r² → g = c/4r² = g/4

Part D — Short Answer

Q10: Both are decreasing curves (hyperbola-shaped) that never reach zero. The inverse square drops much faster: if you double x, an inverse relationship halves y (÷2), but an inverse square quarters y (÷4). The inverse square graph is steeper near the y-axis and flatter further out.
Q11: The student confused (2x)² with 2x². When x doubles, you substitute 2x into the formula: y = c(2x)² = c·4x² = 4cx² — not 2cx². So doubling x quadruples y in a quadratic relationship. The student incorrectly wrote 2x² instead of (2x)² = 4x².
