Part D — Extended Response

Question 10: "B = 0.5 T to the right" describes the x-component only. In a 3D situation, the field could also have a y-component (up/down) or z-component (into/out of page). If the field is purely in the x-direction and we are looking at a flat 2D diagram, the description may be complete. But in a 3D context — e.g., a magnetic field around a wire — the field often has a z-component, which would be shown using ⊙ or ⊗ symbols on the diagram.
Question 11: A dimension is an independent direction of motion — any movement along one axis cannot move you along another. Each axis must be at 90° to all others because this is the definition of independence. If two axes were NOT at 90°, moving along one would automatically create motion along the other — they would no longer be independent directions. You would not truly have an additional degree of freedom.
Question 12: Accept any two good reasons, e.g.: (1) The symbols are compact and fit inside a diagram cell — words would take too much space. (2) The symbols are universal across languages — every physicist in the world recognizes ⊙ and ⊗ regardless of language. (3) The symbols are visually intuitive — they literally look like what they represent (arrowhead / tail feathers). (4) They can be drawn quickly in a grid to show a field pattern over a whole region.
Question 13:
(a) Region A (left): fill with ⊗ symbols; Region B (right): fill with ⊙ symbols.
(b) Current is flowing UPWARD out of the page. Reasoning: the pattern ⊗ on the left and ⊙ on the right matches a current flowing out of the page — the magnetic field circles the wire, going into the page on the left and out on the right.
(c) The symbols would NOT need to change. The same ⊗ and ⊙ pattern would remain — doubling the current doubles the strength (magnitude) of the field but does not change its direction. The symbols only convey direction, not magnitude.
