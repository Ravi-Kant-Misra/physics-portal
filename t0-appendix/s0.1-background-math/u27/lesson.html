3D Vectors

Stepping beyond the flat page — describing vectors in three-dimensional space

🌐

What Is a Dimension?

So far we have only worked with vectors in 2 dimensions, but we live in a 3D world!

A "dimension" is an axis of motion that you can move along — and every axis is at exactly 90° to all other axes. Think of dimensions as independent directions: no amount of moving along one axis can move you along another.

1 Dimension
A Line
You can only move left or right. One axis: x
x-axis only
2 Dimensions
A Flat Plane
Left-right AND up-down. Two axes: x and y
x-axis + y-axis
3 Dimensions
Full Space
Left-right, up-down, AND in/out of page. Three axes: x, y, z
x + y + z axes
📺

The 3rd Axis — The z-Axis

The Laptop Screen Analogy

Think of a laptop screen. The x-axis goes left and right across the screen. The y-axis goes up and down on the screen. Both x and y live entirely on the flat surface of the screen.

Now imagine something poking through the screen toward your face — that direction is the z-axis (positive z). And if something goes away from you into the screen, that is negative z.

The z-axis is always perpendicular (90°) to the flat x-y plane. It is the axis that takes us from 2D into true 3D space.

The key insight: In 2D physics problems, x and y are enough. But in 3D — especially with magnetic fields — vectors can point into or out of the page/screen. We need a way to draw these on a flat piece of paper, which is where the special dot and cross notation comes in.

✦

The Dot and Cross Notation

⊙
Coming OUT of the Page
A dot inside a circle represents a vector pointing toward you — out of the page / screen.
Arrow tip pointing at you
⊗
Going INTO the Page
A cross inside a circle represents a vector pointing away from you — into the page / screen.
Arrow tail feathers going away
Why dot = coming toward you / cross = going away

Memory Trick — Think of an Arrow in Flight

Imagine shooting an arrow directly at you. All you can see is the arrowhead — a sharp point — coming toward your face. That is the dot (⊙).

Now imagine the same arrow flying away from you into the distance. What do you see? The tail feathers — which look like an X or cross. That is the cross (⊗).

🧲

Application — Magnetic Fields

Why 3D Notation?
In IB Physics, magnetic fields often point into or out of the page — especially around current-carrying wires. The dot/cross notation lets us draw these on a flat diagram.
Current-Carrying Wire
When electric current flows through a wire, it creates a magnetic field that circles around the wire in rings — like invisible hoops around the wire.
Left of the Wire
On the LEFT side of a wire carrying current upward out of the page, the magnetic field points INTO the page: shown as a grid of crosses (✕✕✕).
Right of the Wire
On the RIGHT side of that same wire, the magnetic field points OUT OF the page: shown as a grid of dots (•••).

Important note for IB Physics: We will mostly use the 3D dot/cross notation for magnetic fields. We will NOT be doing complex 3-dimensional vector math (adding x+y+z components) in this course. The notation is the key skill to master.

When you see a magnetic field diagram in an IB exam question, look for the ⊙ and ⊗ symbols — they immediately tell you the 3D direction of the field at every point shown.
