Inverse Trig Functions

Use sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, and tan⁻¹ to find unknown angles from side ratios

What Is an Inverse Function?

The Big Idea: Flip the Input and Output

A regular function takes an input x and produces an output y. An inverse function does the opposite — it takes in that y and gives you back the original x.

We write the inverse of a function f as f⁻¹. The superscript ⁻¹ does not mean "1 divided by f" — it means "reverse of f".

Input
x
→
f(x) = y
Output
y
→
f⁻¹(y) = x
Back to
x

Worked Example: y = 5x + 2

The function y = 5x + 2 takes a number, multiplies by 5, then adds 2. Its inverse y = (x − 2)/5 undoes those steps in reverse order: subtract 2 first, then divide by 5.

Notice how the inputs and outputs are swapped:

The outputs of y = 5x + 2 become the inputs of the inverse, and vice versa. The columns are simply swapped!

Regular Trig vs. Inverse Trig

What does each one take in — and give out?

Regular trig functions (sin, cos, tan) take in an angle and output a ratio of two sides.

Inverse trig functions (sin⁻¹, cos⁻¹, tan⁻¹) take in a ratio of two sides and output an angle.

Regular Trig
ANGLE → RATIO
sin(30°) = 0.5
You know the angle — you find the ratio
Inverse Trig
RATIO → ANGLE
sin⁻¹(0.5) = 30°
You know the ratio — you find the angle
Trig Function (angle → ratio) ⇄ Inverse Trig (ratio → angle)
sin(θ) = Opposite / Hypotenuse ⇄ sin⁻¹(Opposite / Hypotenuse) = θ
cos(θ) = Adjacent / Hypotenuse ⇄ cos⁻¹(Adjacent / Hypotenuse) = θ
tan(θ) = Opposite / Adjacent ⇄ tan⁻¹(Opposite / Adjacent) = θ
🔑
When to use inverse trig: Whenever you know two sides of a right triangle and need to find an angle, use the appropriate inverse trig function on your calculator. On most calculators, press SHIFT (or 2nd) before sin, cos, or tan.

Which Inverse Trig to Use?

Use sin⁻¹ when you have
Opp + Hyp
θ = sin⁻¹(Opp/Hyp)
Use cos⁻¹ when you have
Adj + Hyp
θ = cos⁻¹(Adj/Hyp)
Use tan⁻¹ when you have
Opp + Adj
θ = tan⁻¹(Opp/Adj)
