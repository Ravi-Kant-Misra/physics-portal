Uncertainty Notation

Learn how to express measurements honestly — using the a ± Δa notation and the range of possible values

±

The Notation: a ± Δa

Standard Uncertainty Notation

a
Number value
±
Δa
Uncertainty

This notation tells us the actual value could be anywhere in the range from (a − Δa) to (a + Δa). The uncertainty Δa is always positive.

Visualising the Range — Example: 5 ± 3

The measurement 5 ± 3 means the true value could be anywhere between 2 and 8. Numbers like 2, 3.14159, and 7 are all possible values. Numbers like 1 or 8.001 are not possible values.

a−Δa = 2 a = 5 a+Δa = 8

Converting Between Notation and Range

Given a ± Δa → Find the range

Min = a − Δa
Max = a + Δa

Example: 12 ± 7
Min = 12 − 7 = 5
Max = 12 + 7 = 19
Range: 5 to 19

Given range → Find a ± Δa

a = (max + min) / 2
Δa = (max − min) / 2

Example: range 2 to 8
a = (8+2)/2 = 5
Δa = (8−2)/2 = 3
Result: 5 ± 3

Why these formulas work

a is the average (midpoint) of the range — halfway between max and min. Δa is half the total width of the range. Together they reconstruct the full ± interval symmetrically around the centre value.
