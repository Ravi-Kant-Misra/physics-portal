Review of Trig — Finding Missing Sides

Master the 3-step rearrangement method to solve for any unknown side in a right triangle

△

The Big Idea

In physics, vectors constantly appear as right triangles. You are often given one side and one angle and must find a missing side — the opposite, adjacent, or hypotenuse. Trigonometry gives you the tool to do this.

The key skill is not just memorising the three ratios (Sin, Cos, Tan) — it is knowing how to pick the right one and then rearrange it algebraically to isolate the unknown. That is what this unit is all about.

Labeling a Right Triangle

The labels O, A, H are always defined relative to the angle θ — they change depending on which angle you choose.

📰

The Three Trig Ratios — Choosing the Right One

Sin(θ)
= O / H
Opposite & Hypotenuse
Cos(θ)
= A / H
Adjacent & Hypotenuse
Tan(θ)
= O / A
Opposite & Adjacent

Use SOH CAH TOA to remember: look at which two sides are involved, then pick the ratio that contains BOTH of them.

SOH = Sin(Opposite/Hypotenuse)  |  CAH = Cos(Adjacent/Hypotenuse)  |  TOA = Tan(Opposite/Adjacent)
📝

The 3-Step Rearrangement Method

3-Step Method — Solving for a Missing Side

1
Choose the identity Look at which two sides are involved (the one you know and the one you want). The trig ratio that contains BOTH of those sides is the one you use.
2
Rearrange Rearrange the equation so the unknown side is alone on one side. Use algebra — multiply or divide both sides until the unknown is isolated.
3
Substitute and solve Plug in the numbers you know and calculate the answer. Include units if applicable.

Complete Reference — All 6 Rearrangement Forms

Original Identity Rearranged: Find O Rearranged: Find A or H
Sin(θ) = O/H
Use when: O and H involved		 O = H · Sin(θ) H = O / Sin(θ)
Cos(θ) = A/H
Use when: A and H involved		 A = H · Cos(θ) H = A / Cos(θ)
Tan(θ) = O/A
Use when: O and A involved		 O = A · Tan(θ) A = O / Tan(θ)

Algebra reminder: If the unknown is in the numerator, multiply both sides by the denominator to isolate it. If the unknown is in the denominator, multiply both sides by the unknown then divide both sides by the trig value.

🔎

Worked Examples

Example 5 Find Hypotenuse using Cos
θ = 15° Adjacent = 50 Find: Hypotenuse
Step 1 — Choose the identity
I have: Adjacent (A) and want: Hypotenuse (H)
Cos has both A and H → use Cos
Step 2 — Rearrange
Cos(θ) = A / H
H · Cos(θ) = A
H = A / Cos(θ)
Step 3 — Substitute and solve
H = 50 / Cos(15°)
H = 50 / 0.97
H = 51.5
The hypotenuse is always longer than any other side. Getting 51.5 > 50 confirms this makes sense!
Example 6 Find Opposite using Tan
θ = 15° Adjacent = 50 Find: Opposite
Step 1 — Choose the identity
I have: Adjacent (A) and want: Opposite (O)
Tan has both O and A → use Tan
Step 2 — Rearrange
Tan(θ) = O / A
A · Tan(θ) = O
O = A · Tan(θ)
Step 3 — Substitute and solve
O = 50 · Tan(15°)
O = 50 × 0.27
O = 13.5
Notice: same triangle, same angle, but a different question. A small angle (15°) means the opposite side is much shorter than the adjacent — 13.5 vs 50 makes sense!
Example 7 Find Opposite using Sin
θ = 30° Hypotenuse = 20 Find: Opposite
Step 1
Have: H = 20  |  Want: O → use Sin (connects O and H)
Step 2 — Rearrange
Sin(θ) = O/H  →  O = H · Sin(θ)
Step 3 — Solve
O = 20 · Sin(30°) = 20 × 0.50
O = 10
Example 8 Find Adjacent using Cos
θ = 45° Hypotenuse = 14 Find: Adjacent
Step 1
Have: H = 14  |  Want: A → use Cos (connects A and H)
Step 2 — Rearrange
Cos(θ) = A/H  →  A = H · Cos(θ)
Step 3 — Solve
A = 14 · Cos(45°) = 14 × 0.707
A = 9.9
Example 9 Find Opposite using Tan
θ = 60° Adjacent = 8 Find: Opposite
Step 1
Have: A = 8  |  Want: O → use Tan (connects O and A)
Step 2 — Rearrange
Tan(θ) = O/A  →  O = A · Tan(θ)
Step 3 — Solve
O = 8 · Tan(60°) = 8 × 1.73
O = 13.9
Example 10 Find Hypotenuse using Sin
θ = 25° Opposite = 6 Find: Hypotenuse
Step 1
Have: O = 6  |  Want: H → use Sin (connects O and H)
Step 2 — Rearrange
Sin(θ) = O/H  →  H · Sin(θ) = O  →  H = O / Sin(θ)
Step 3 — Solve
H = 6 / Sin(25°) = 6 / 0.423
H = 14.2
When the unknown is in the denominator, you must divide by the trig value — not multiply. The hypotenuse is always the largest side, so 14.2 > 6 confirms the answer.
💡

Key Tips & Checks

Sanity Check — Is Your Answer Reasonable?

The hypotenuse is always the longest side. If you solve for H and get a number smaller than O or A, you made an error. Similarly, if the angle is small (close to 0°), the opposite side should be small too.

Calculator Mode

Always make sure your calculator is in DEGREE mode, not radians. If Sin(30°) gives you anything other than 0.5, check the mode setting.

Which Rearrangement to Use?

Ask: is the unknown on the top (numerator) or bottom (denominator) of the fraction? If top, multiply. If bottom, divide. For example: Sin(θ) = O/H — if you want H (denominator), divide: H = O/Sin(θ).
