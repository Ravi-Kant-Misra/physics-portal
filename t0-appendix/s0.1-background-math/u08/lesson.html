Absolute, Fractional & Percent Uncertainty

Three ways to express the same uncertainty — and how to convert between them

🤔

Why Three Types?

When you report a measurement, you always include an uncertainty — a range that captures how precise your instrument is. For example, a ruler might give you 4 ± 1 cm.

That "± 1" can be written in three equivalent ways: as a raw number (absolute), as a fraction of the measurement (fractional), or as a percentage (percent). They all say the same thing — just in different languages.

Knowing how to switch between them is essential, because different calculations and reporting conventions require different forms.

🔁
4 ± 1 cm
= 4 cm ± 0.25
= 4 cm ± 25%
All three say the same thing!
📏
Absolute Uncertainty
Δa, Δb, Δc
4 ± 1
Expressed as a plain number, independent of the original measurement. Same units as the measurement.
➗
Fractional Uncertainty
Δa/a, Δb/b, Δc/c
4 ± 0.25
Expressed as the ratio of the absolute uncertainty to the measurement. Dimensionless — no units.
📊
Percent Uncertainty
(Δa/a)×100%
4 ± 25%
Expressed as the fractional uncertainty multiplied by 100. Makes the size of the error easy to grasp at a glance.

Key Insight — All Three Are Equivalent

Absolute: 4 ± 1
⟺
Fractional: 4 ± 0.25
⟺
Percent: 4 ± 25%
All three express the same underlying measurement and the same uncertainty — only the notation differs.
🔄

Converting Between Forms

➡️
Absolute → Fractional: divide by the measurement
Δa (absolute)
÷ a →
Δa/a (fractional)
1
Take the absolute uncertainty and divide by the measured value.
2
Example: absolute = 1, measurement = 4 → fractional = 1 ÷ 4 = 0.25
The fractional uncertainty tells you what fraction of the measurement your uncertainty represents. 0.25 means your uncertainty is 1/4 of the value.
⬅️
Fractional → Absolute: multiply by the measurement
Δa/a (fractional)
× a →
Δa (absolute)
1
Multiply the fractional uncertainty by the measured value.
2
Example: fractional = 0.25, measurement = 4 → absolute = 0.25 × 4 = 1
This reverses the first conversion — you recover the original raw uncertainty with units.
📊
Fractional → Percent: multiply by 100
Δa/a (fractional)
× 100 →
% uncertainty
1
Multiply the fractional uncertainty by 100 to get the percent.
2
Example: fractional = 0.25 → percent = 0.25 × 100 = 25%
This is the most intuitive form. "25% uncertainty" is easy to visualise — your uncertainty is 25 cents of every dollar measured.
🔢
Percent → Fractional: divide by 100
% uncertainty
÷ 100 →
Δa/a (fractional)
1
Divide the percent uncertainty by 100.
2
Example: percent = 25% → fractional = 25 ÷ 100 = 0.25
Once you have the fractional form, you can then multiply by the measurement to get the absolute uncertainty if needed.
🏷️

Unit Placement Rule

📌 Where the unit goes tells you which type it is

Absolute Uncertainty
4.00 ± 0.25 kg
Unit after the uncertainty. Both the value and the uncertainty carry the unit.
✅ "4.00 kg, uncertain by 0.25 kg"
Fractional Uncertainty
4.00 kg ± 0.25
Unit after the number only. The uncertainty has no unit — it is a pure ratio.
✅ "4.00 kg, uncertain by 0.25 of the value"
✂️

Rounding Rules for Uncertainty

Rule 1
If the absolute uncertainty has MORE decimal places than the number → round the NUMBER to match the uncertainty.
7.55 ± 0.4
→
7.6 ± 0.4
The uncertainty is to 1 decimal place, so the number must also be reported to 1 decimal place.
Rule 1 — Another Example
Round the number to match the decimal places of the uncertainty.
1874 ± 40
→
1870 ± 40
Uncertainty is to the tens place, so the number is rounded to the tens place.
Rule 2
If the absolute uncertainty has FEWER decimal places than the number → round the UNCERTAINTY to match the number.
3.2574 ± 0.1
→
3.3 ± 0.1
The uncertainty is only to 1 decimal place; report the number to 1 decimal place.
Rule 2 — Another Example
Always match the precision of number and uncertainty.
3.25 ± 0.0435
→
3.25 ± 0.04
The number has 2 decimal places, so the uncertainty is rounded to 2 decimal places.
Warning — Extreme Case
When the uncertainty is larger than the number's precision, the number effectively becomes zero at that decimal place.
0.042 ± 0.5
→
0.0 ± 0.5
The uncertainty (±0.5) is so large relative to 0.042 that the number rounds to 0.0. This signals the measurement has very poor precision.

Worked Examples

Example 1
Absolute → Fractional → Percent
5 m ± 3
1
Fractional = absolute ÷ measurement = 3 ÷ 5 = 0.6
2
Percent = fractional × 100 = 0.6 × 100 = 60%
Absolute:5 m ± 3 Fractional:5 m ± 0.6 Percent:5 m ± 60%
Example 2
Voltage — Absolute → Percent
20 V ± 2
1
Fractional = 2 ÷ 20 = 0.1
2
Percent = 0.1 × 100 = 10%
Absolute:20 V ± 2 Percent:20 V ± 10%
Example 3
Current — Percent Uncertainty
9 A ± 0.35
1
Fractional = 0.35 ÷ 9 = 0.0389…
2
Percent = 0.0389 × 100 ≈ 3.9% → round to 4%
Absolute:9 A ± 0.35 Percent:9 A ± 4%
Note: the percent was rounded to a sensible number of significant figures (1 s.f. for a rough uncertainty). Keeping 3.89% would imply more precision in the uncertainty than the measurement warrants.
Example 4
Rounding — Match decimal places
3.25 ± 0.0435
1
The number (3.25) has 2 decimal places.
2
Round uncertainty to 2 d.p.: 0.0435 → 0.04
Correctly written:3.25 ± 0.04
Example 5
Rounding — Number must match uncertainty
3.2574 ± 0.1
1
The uncertainty (0.1) has 1 decimal place.
2
Round the number to 1 d.p.: 3.2574 → 3.3
Correctly written:3.3 ± 0.1
