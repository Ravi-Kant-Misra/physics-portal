Reference — Conversions & Rules

Absolute → Fractional: Δa / a
Fractional → Absolute: (Δa/a) × a
Fractional → Percent: (Δa/a) × 100%
Percent → Fractional: % / 100
Rounding reminder: Match decimal places — if the uncertainty has more d.p., round the number; if fewer d.p., round the uncertainty. Unit after the uncertainty = absolute. Unit after the number only = fractional.

Part C — True or False

#13: TRUE — Unit (m) follows both value and uncertainty. That is absolute form.
#14: FALSE — Fractional and percent uncertainties are dimensionless (no units). Only absolute uncertainty shares the unit.
#15: FALSE — You must first convert to fractional (÷ measurement), then multiply by 100. Multiplying the absolute uncertainty by 100 directly gives a meaningless number.
#16: FALSE — "30 kg ± 0.06" has the unit only after 30. The 0.06 has no unit — it is a fractional uncertainty (meaning 6% of 30 kg = 1.8 kg absolute uncertainty).
#17: TRUE — Percent = fractional × 100 = 0.08 × 100 = 8%.
#18: TRUE — When uncertainty has fewer d.p. than the number, round the number to match. The uncertainty sets the precision limit.

Part D — Extended Response

Q19a. Fractional = 3/47 ≈ 0.0638. Percent = 0.0638 × 100 ≈ 6.4%.
Q19b. New fractional = 0.5/47 ≈ 0.01064. New percent ≈ 1.1%. Decrease = 6.4% − 1.1% = 5.3 percentage points. The better instrument reduced the percent uncertainty from about 6.4% to about 1.1%.

Q20. When two measurements have the same percent uncertainty, they share the same relative precision — the uncertainty is the same fraction of each measurement — but because the measurements are different sizes, the raw (absolute) uncertainty values are different. For example: 10 m ± 1 m (10% uncertainty) and 500 m ± 50 m (also 10% uncertainty). Both are "equally uncertain" in relative terms, but 50 m is a much larger gap than 1 m in absolute terms. The percent form lets you compare precision across quantities of different magnitudes.
