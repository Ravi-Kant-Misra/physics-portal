Chapter 9 · Nuclear Physics

Lise Meitner

"She explained nuclear fission — one of the most important discoveries in human history. Her male colleague received the Nobel Prize. Her name was barely mentioned."

1878 – 1968 Nuclear Fission IB Theme 5 Austria / Sweden

Entering a World That Did Not Want Her

Lise Meitner was born in Vienna in 1878 into a Jewish family. She was brilliant from childhood, but Austria did not allow women to attend university at the time. She studied privately, passed the entrance exam for the University of Vienna, and was admitted in 1901 — one of the first women to be allowed in. She earned her doctorate in physics in 1906 — only the second woman to do so at the University of Vienna.

She moved to Berlin to study with Max Planck, but the University of Berlin did not allow women in laboratories. She worked for two years without pay or official status, studying in a basement room designated as a "carpenter's workshop" because it was not technically a proper laboratory.

Thirty Years of Collaboration with Hahn

Despite these obstacles, she eventually gained official recognition and spent nearly 30 years working at the Kaiser Wilhelm Institute in Berlin in a close scientific partnership with chemist Otto Hahn. Together they discovered the element protactinium (1918). Their collaboration was scientifically extraordinary and personally warm — a rare friendship across the strict social divisions of the era.

Lise Meitner, one of the pioneers of nuclear physics. Element 109, Meitnerium, was named in her honour.

Flight from Nazi Germany

In 1933, the Nazis came to power. Jewish scientists were systematically removed from their positions. Meitner had Austrian citizenship and was initially protected. But in 1938, Germany annexed Austria. She was now a German Jew — in immediate danger.

She had no passport valid for exit. In July 1938, two Dutch physicists helped her slip across the border into the Netherlands with only 10 Marks in her pocket. She had left behind nearly everything — her books, her instruments, her home of 30 years. She eventually settled in Sweden at the Nobel Institute in Stockholm, where she continued her research.

In Sweden, she worked in isolation. She spoke almost no Swedish, had little equipment compared to Berlin, and received no research support for years. She kept working. She corresponded constantly with Hahn, who had remained in Germany.

The Discovery of Nuclear Fission (1938)

In December 1938, Hahn wrote to Meitner with a puzzling experimental result: when uranium was bombarded with neutrons, the products appeared to include barium — an element with roughly half the atomic number of uranium. How could bombarding uranium produce barium? Hahn suspected a chemical error. He asked Meitner to explain it.

Meitner was on Christmas holiday in Sweden, walking in the snow with her nephew Otto Frisch (also a physicist). They stopped, sat on a log, and worked through the physics.

Their explanation: the uranium nucleus, when struck by a neutron, becomes unstable and splits in two — producing barium, krypton, and several free neutrons. The mass of the products is slightly less than the original uranium. By Einstein's E = mc², this missing mass is converted to an enormous release of energy.

Nuclear Fission — Meitner's Explanation
²³⁵U + n → ¹⁴¹Ba + ⁹²Kr + 3n + ENERGY

The "missing" mass (about 0.1%) is converted to ~200 MeV of kinetic energy per fission event — millions of times more than a chemical reaction.

Meitner and Frisch published the theoretical explanation in January 1939. They coined the term "nuclear fission", borrowing from the biological term for cell division. Within months, physicists around the world had verified the result. Within years, it led to the Manhattan Project and the first nuclear weapons. Within decades, it powered nuclear reactors that supply electricity to hundreds of millions of people today.

The Nobel That Never Came

In 1944, Otto Hahn was awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for "the discovery of the fission of heavy nuclei." Lise Meitner was not included. She had provided the theoretical explanation without which the experimental result would have remained an inexplicable anomaly. She had directed the research programme. She had been Hahn's equal — and in physics, his superior — for 30 years.

She was nominated for the Nobel Prize 48 times. She never received it. Albert Einstein called her "the German Marie Curie." She later said of the Nobel omission that she had made peace with it — though letters reveal she was deeply hurt. She never publicly criticised Hahn for failing to advocate for her inclusion.

"I love physics with my whole heart. It is a kind of personal love, as one has for a person to whom one is grateful for many things."

— Lise Meitner

She declined to participate in the Manhattan Project, saying: "I will have nothing to do with a bomb." This decision cost her professionally in the post-war period, when Manhattan Project participants dominated atomic physics. She never apologised for it.

Timeline

  • 1878 Born in Vienna, Austria, to a Jewish family.
  • 1906 Earns doctorate in physics at University of Vienna — one of the first women to do so.
  • 1907 Moves to Berlin; works unpaid in a basement "workshop" because women are barred from labs.
  • 1918 Co-discovers element protactinium with Otto Hahn.
  • 1926 Becomes Germany's first female physics professor at the University of Berlin.
  • 1933 Loses her professorship under Nazi racial laws but keeps her Kaiser Wilhelm Institute position (Austrian citizenship).
  • 1938 Flees Germany with 10 Marks; settles in Stockholm.
  • 1938–39 Explains nuclear fission with nephew Otto Frisch; publishes theoretical paper.
  • 1944 Hahn alone wins the Nobel Prize for fission. Meitner is not included.
  • 1966 Receives the Enrico Fermi Award (with Hahn and Strassmann) — one of the few official recognitions.
  • 1968 Dies in Cambridge, age 89. Her epitaph: "A physicist who never lost her humanity."
  • 1997 Element 109 named Meitnerium in her honour.
Moral of the Story

Lise Meitner was denied credit, denied access, denied recognition, and denied safety — by an entire social system determined to exclude her from the world she loved. She did not let any of it stop her. She continued to work, to think, to contribute, in basements and in exile and in under-resourced labs. And when the world's most destructive technology emerged from her discovery, she refused to be part of it. Her story teaches us that the measure of a scientist — and a person — is not the prizes they receive, but the integrity and courage with which they pursue what they know to be true and right.
