Einstein & Relativity

"What would you see if you could ride alongside a beam of light? This question, which Einstein asked himself at age 16, eventually bent space and time."

1879 – 1955 Special & General Relativity IB Theme 1 Germany / USA

The Child Who Was Written Off

Albert Einstein was born in Ulm, Germany, in 1879. He did not speak until he was around three years old, and even as a young child spoke slowly and haltingly — a trait that alarmed his parents. A teacher reportedly told his father that "it doesn't matter what field he goes into, he'll never make a success of anything." He failed the entrance exam to the Zurich Polytechnic on his first attempt (at age 15, when he was below the required age).

But Einstein was not stupid. He was bored. He found rote learning pointless and preferred to think for himself. He loved mathematics and physics with deep passion and had an extraordinary ability to think in pictures — what he called Gedankenexperiments (thought experiments). At 16, he imagined riding alongside a beam of light. What would he see? Classical physics had no good answer. That discomfort would eventually lead to special relativity.

The Patent Clerk

After graduating (finally), he could not get a university position — professors whose classes he had skipped would not write him recommendations. He ended up working as a patent examiner in Bern, Switzerland. Far from academia, surrounded by practical inventions rather than theoretical physics, he spent his lunch breaks and evenings doing physics. In 1905, in a single year, he published four papers that transformed physics. He was 26 years old. He was still working at the patent office.

Albert Einstein in 1947. He became the most recognisable scientist in history — and one of the most revolutionary thinkers of any era.

IB Physics Connection

Theme 1 — Relativity: Special relativity is in the IB curriculum. Time dilation, length contraction, and the equivalence of mass and energy (E = mc²) are key concepts. The photoelectric effect (also Einstein 1905) is in Theme 5.

The Miracle Year: 1905

In a single year, Einstein published four groundbreaking papers:

1. The Photoelectric Effect

Light comes in packets (photons). Energy per photon = hf. This won him the Nobel Prize in 1921 and launched quantum mechanics.

2. Brownian Motion

The random jiggling of pollen particles in water proved that atoms and molecules are real physical objects, not just convenient fictions.

3. Special Relativity

Space and time are not fixed — they stretch and compress depending on relative motion. The speed of light is constant for all observers.

4. Mass–Energy Equivalence

Mass and energy are interchangeable: E = mc². A tiny amount of mass contains an enormous amount of energy. The foundation of nuclear physics.

Special Relativity: The Two Postulates

Einstein based his special theory on two simple postulates:

  1. The laws of physics are the same for all observers moving at constant velocity relative to each other. (There is no special "rest frame" for the universe.)
  2. The speed of light in a vacuum is constant for all observers, regardless of their motion or the motion of the source.

From these two statements alone — with mathematics — follow some of the strangest conclusions in the history of science:

  • Time dilation: A moving clock ticks more slowly than a stationary one. The faster you move, the slower time passes for you relative to someone standing still.
  • Length contraction: A moving object is shorter in the direction of motion, as measured by a stationary observer.
  • Mass increase: An object gets harder to accelerate as it approaches the speed of light — as if its mass increases. Nothing with mass can reach the speed of light.
E = mc²

Mass and energy are different forms of the same thing. The c² (the square of the speed of light, ≈ 9×10¹⁶ m²/s²) is a huge number — so even a tiny amount of mass contains a colossal amount of energy. This is why nuclear reactions release so much energy from so little material.

General Relativity: Gravity Is Not a Force

Special relativity dealt with uniform motion. In 1915, after 10 years of work, Einstein published his general theory of relativity — which extended the principles to accelerating frames and gave a completely new theory of gravity.

Newton had described gravity as a force acting at a distance. Einstein described it as the curvature of spacetime. Mass and energy warp the geometry of space and time around them. Other objects (including light) follow curved paths through this warped spacetime — not because a force is pulling them, but because the straightest possible path through curved spacetime is a curve.

The Sun's mass warps spacetime, and Earth follows a curved orbit not because the Sun is pulling on it, but because it is rolling along the curved spacetime geometry like a marble on a curved sheet.

"Imagination is more important than knowledge. Knowledge is limited. Imagination encircles the world."

— Albert Einstein

General relativity predicted that light would bend around massive objects. This was confirmed dramatically during the 1919 solar eclipse, when Arthur Eddington measured the apparent positions of stars near the Sun and found they were shifted exactly as Einstein's equations predicted. The next day, Einstein was on the front page of every newspaper in the world.

Timeline

  • 1879 Born in Ulm, Germany.
  • 1895 Fails entrance exam for ETH Zurich. Attends secondary school in Aarau, Switzerland instead.
  • 1900 Graduates from ETH Zurich. Cannot get academic position; becomes patent clerk in Bern.
  • 1905 The annus mirabilis — four landmark papers published.
  • 1915 Publishes general theory of relativity.
  • 1919 Eddington's eclipse expedition confirms light bending — Einstein becomes world famous overnight.
  • 1921 Nobel Prize in Physics (for the photoelectric effect, not relativity).
  • 1933 Flees Nazi Germany; emigrates to the USA; joins the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton.
  • 1955 Dies in Princeton, age 76. Leaves behind a unified field theory he never completed.
Moral of the Story

Einstein was written off repeatedly. He failed exams. He was too difficult to employ. He was working in a patent office when he rewrote physics. His secret was not that he was smarter than everyone else — it was that he refused to accept a description of the universe that didn't feel right, and he had the imagination and persistence to keep asking why. The most powerful tool in science is not a calculator — it is a question that you refuse to stop asking.
