Isaac Newton

"He didn't just discover gravity — he showed that the force pulling an apple to the ground is the same force holding the Moon in orbit. The universe was suddenly one."

1643 – 1727 Gravity & Motion IB Theme 1 England

A Difficult Beginning

Isaac Newton was born on Christmas Day, 1643, in Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire, England. He was premature and so small at birth that his mother said he could fit inside a quart pot. His father had died three months before he was born. When Newton was three, his mother remarried and left him in the care of his grandmother. He reportedly hated his stepfather and, by his own account, once threatened to burn down the house with his stepfather and mother inside it.

He was a troubled, lonely child who found refuge in making things: kites, windmills, water clocks, sundials. He was not immediately recognised as brilliant at school. His uncle eventually noticed his ability and pushed for him to go to Cambridge.

The Miraculous Years (1665–1667)

In 1665, Cambridge was closed because of plague. Newton went home to Woolsthorpe. In the next 18 months — alone, in the countryside — he invented calculus, began working out the theory of gravity, and made fundamental discoveries about the nature of light. He was 23 years old. These two years are known as his annus mirabilis — the miraculous years. He later said: "All this was in the two plague years of 1665 and 1666, for in those days I was in the prime of my age for invention."

Isaac Newton, painted by Godfrey Kneller in 1689, two years after Principia Mathematica was published.

IB Physics Connection

Theme 1 — Space, Time & Motion: Newton's three laws of motion and his law of universal gravitation are the direct content of IB Theme 1. Circular motion, momentum, and energy all build on his framework.

Newton's Three Laws of Motion

These three laws, published in the Principia in 1687, are the engine of classical physics. Every IB student must know them deeply.

I
Law of Inertia
An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted upon by an external net force.
II
F = ma
The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration. The greater the force, the greater the change in motion.
III
Action & Reaction
For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always come in pairs.

Universal Gravitation: One Law Rules Them All

The famous apple story is probably embellished, but Newton himself confirmed that seeing a falling apple in his garden made him wonder: does the same force that pulls the apple down also pull the Moon? If so, the Moon is constantly falling toward Earth — it just moves sideways fast enough that it keeps missing.

His Law of Universal Gravitation states: every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. In mathematical form:

F = G · m₁m₂ / r²

The astonishing thing was that the same equation described a falling apple, the orbit of the Moon, the orbit of Jupiter, and the tides. For the first time in history, earthly and heavenly physics were unified into one framework.

Newton on Light: The Prism Experiments

Newton also made fundamental discoveries about light. He passed sunlight through a glass prism and saw it split into a rainbow spectrum. Then he passed the coloured light through a second prism and recombined it into white light again. His conclusion: white light is not pure — it is a mixture of all colours. Each colour bends by a slightly different angle in glass (dispersion), which is why rainbows exist.

He proposed that light was made of tiny particles he called corpuscles. This put him in direct conflict with Christiaan Huygens, who argued light was a wave. This debate — particle or wave? — would run for two more centuries. (See Chapter 4.)

Timeline

  • 1643 Born at Woolsthorpe Manor, Lincolnshire, England.
  • 1661 Enters Trinity College, Cambridge.
  • 1665–67 Plague closes Cambridge; annus mirabilis — calculus, gravity, optics.
  • 1669 Appointed Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge (the chair later held by Stephen Hawking).
  • 1672 Presents reflecting telescope to the Royal Society; publishes first paper on light and colour.
  • 1687 Publishes Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica — three laws of motion + universal gravitation.
  • 1704 Publishes Opticks — his work on light and colour.
  • 1705 Knighted by Queen Anne — the first scientist to receive this honour.
  • 1727 Dies in London, age 84. Buried in Westminster Abbey.
Moral of the Story

Newton was a difficult, secretive, and often unpleasant person who had bitter rivalries and did not share credit easily. He was also perhaps the greatest scientific mind in history. His story teaches us that genius does not come packaged with good character — and that the most important breakthroughs sometimes come not from conferences and collaboration, but from a young person alone in a room with time to think. Isolation, curiosity, and patience can be the most powerful laboratory of all.
