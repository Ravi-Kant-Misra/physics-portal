Marie & Pierre Curie

"She was told women could not be scientists. She won two Nobel Prizes in two different sciences — something no other person has ever done."

A Woman Who Was Not Supposed to Be a Scientist

Maria Sklodowska was born in Warsaw, Poland, in 1867, when Poland was under Russian occupation. Higher education for women was forbidden. She and her sister attended the clandestine "Flying University" — a secret school that met in private homes, changing location each week to avoid the authorities. This early experience of learning under oppression would define her relationship with knowledge for the rest of her life: fierce, determined, and non-negotiable.

She worked as a governess for years, sending her earnings to Paris to support her sister's medical studies, on the agreement that her sister would later support her. At 24, she finally moved to Paris and enrolled at the Sorbonne — one of the few women in the faculty of science. She lived in a tiny, freezing attic, sometimes too poor to eat. She earned her degree in physics first in her class, then her second degree in mathematics second in her class.

Meeting Pierre

She met Pierre Curie in 1894 through a colleague. He was a distinguished physicist who had already discovered piezoelectricity. He was immediately struck by her intellectual seriousness and fell in love. Their relationship was a true scientific partnership — rare for the time. Pierre offered to give up his own research to assist hers. She persuaded him instead to pursue his doctorate so they could work as equals.

Marie Curie c.1920. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, and remains the only person to have won Nobel Prizes in two different sciences.

The Discovery of Radioactivity

In 1896, Henri Becquerel had discovered that uranium emitted a mysterious penetrating radiation. Marie chose this as her doctoral research topic — a bold choice; no one else was working on it. Using a sensitive electrometer designed by Pierre, she measured the ionising power of rays from different uranium compounds. Her key insight: the amount of radiation was proportional only to the quantity of uranium present, not to the chemical form it was in. The radiation was an atomic property — not a chemical one.

She coined the term "radioactivity". She then found that thorium was also radioactive. Even more surprisingly, when she tested pitchblende (uranium ore), it was more radioactive than its uranium content alone would explain. She concluded there must be an unknown radioactive element in the ore.

Pierre abandoned his own research and joined her. Working in a leaky shed — their laboratory — they processed tonnes of pitchblende by hand over four years. They isolated two new elements: polonium (named after Marie's homeland, Poland) and radium.

"One never notices what has been done; one can only see what remains to be done."

— Marie Curie, in a letter to her brother, 1894

Two Nobel Prizes

1903
Nobel Prize in Physics
Shared with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel, for their research on radiation. At first, only Pierre and Becquerel were nominated — Pierre insisted Marie be included.
1911
Nobel Prize in Chemistry
Awarded solely to Marie, for the discovery of polonium and radium. She became the first person ever to win two Nobel Prizes in different sciences.

The 1911 Nobel came during a period of intense personal difficulty. Pierre had died in 1906 — struck by a horse-drawn carriage in Paris and killed instantly. Marie was devastated. She took over his professorship at the Sorbonne, becoming the first female professor there, and continued their joint research alone. The year she won her second Nobel, the French press was attacking her for an alleged affair. The Nobel committee suggested she not attend the ceremony. She attended anyway.

Timeline

  • 1867 Marie (Maria Sklodowska) born in Warsaw, Poland.
  • 1883 Graduates top of her secondary school class despite restricted access to education.
  • 1891 Moves to Paris; enrolls at the Sorbonne.
  • 1894 Meets Pierre Curie; they begin their scientific collaboration and relationship.
  • 1895 Marries Pierre. She publishes her study of magnetic properties of steel.
  • 1898 Announces discovery of polonium (July) and radium (December).
  • 1903 First Nobel Prize in Physics. First woman to receive a Nobel Prize.
  • 1906 Pierre killed in road accident. Marie takes over his professorship — first female professor at the Sorbonne.
  • 1911 Second Nobel Prize in Chemistry. Only person ever to win Nobels in two different sciences.
  • 1914–18 Develops mobile X-ray units ("petites Curies") for WWI field hospitals; personally drives them to the front.
  • 1934 Dies of aplastic anaemia caused by decades of radiation exposure. Age 66.

The Cost of Discovery

Marie Curie died from aplastic anaemia — a blood disorder caused by her decades of exposure to radiation. She and Pierre had worked with radioactive materials with no protection because no one yet knew how dangerous they were. Her personal notebooks are still so radioactive today that they are stored in lead-lined boxes in Paris, and researchers who wish to read them must sign a waiver acknowledging the radiation risk.

She did not complain. She did not stop. She continued working almost until her death, saying she found the laboratory "the only place where I do not feel lost."

Moral of the Story

Marie Curie was told at every stage that the world she wanted to inhabit was not for her: not for a woman, not for a foreigner, not for someone without resources. She entered every closed door anyway — and found that dedication, precision, and the willingness to endure discomfort for the sake of knowledge eventually open every door. Her story is not just about radioactivity. It is about refusing to accept that the rules of the room apply to you when you know your purpose.
