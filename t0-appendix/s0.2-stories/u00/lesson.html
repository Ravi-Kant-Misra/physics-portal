Chapter 0 — Introduction

Welcome to Physics

Before we solve a single equation, let's understand the greatest adventure in human history — the quest to understand how the universe works.

What Is Physics, Really?

Physics is not a book of rules. It is a set of stories — stories told by people who refused to accept "that's just the way it is." They watched the sky, dropped objects off towers, bent light through glass, and asked a question that changed everything: why?

Every formula you will learn has a person behind it. That person had doubts, made mistakes, sometimes was laughed at, sometimes was punished — and yet kept going. This course is about those people as much as it is about their equations.

"Physics is the only profession in which prophecy is not only accurate but routine."

— Neil deGrasse Tyson

Over the next chapters, we will meet 10 scientists whose work forms the backbone of IB Physics. Each chapter tells a human story: the struggle, the breakthrough, and the lesson it holds for you today.

The IB Physics Curriculum at a Glance

IB Physics is organised into five themes, moving from the everyday world you can see and touch — to the invisible, abstract world of particles, quantum states, and radioactivity.

Theme 1
Space, Time & Motion
Motion, forces, momentum, energy, gravity, relativity
Theme 2
Particulate Nature of Matter
Kinetic theory, thermal energy, gas laws, phase changes
Theme 3
Wave Behaviour
Waves, sound, light, optics, diffraction, interference
Theme 4
Fields
Gravitational, electric, magnetic fields and their interactions
Theme 5
Nuclear & Quantum Physics
Radioactivity, nuclear reactions, photoelectric effect, quantum theory

We start at the concrete and familiar (a ball thrown through the air) and journey toward the deeply abstract (a cat that is both alive and dead; particles that share statistics). The scientists in our story chapters map directly onto this journey.

How Science Actually Works

Before we begin the stories, here is something important to understand: science does not work the way textbooks suggest.

  • It is messy — full of wrong turns, abandoned hypotheses, and accidental discoveries.
  • It is social — ideas are accepted or rejected partly because of who proposes them, not just the evidence.
  • It is cumulative — every generation builds on (and sometimes demolishes) the work of the last.
  • It is human — scientists have rivalries, make errors, face prejudice, and sometimes lose credit for their own discoveries.

Every chapter in this series illustrates one or more of these realities. By the end, you will understand not just the physics — but the remarkable human process through which we came to know it.

"Science is not a collection of facts. It is a way of thinking."

— Carl Sagan

Three Big Ideas That Connect Everything

1. Observation beats authority

From Galileo's telescope to Rosalind Franklin's X-ray images, the stories of science are stories of evidence overcoming assumption. The universe does not care what we believe it should do.

2. Mathematics is the language of nature

Newton gave us calculus to describe motion. Maxwell gave us equations that predicted electromagnetic waves. Einstein gave us field equations that predicted black holes. Every one of these predictions was later verified by experiment. Mathematics is not abstract decoration — it is physics in its most precise form.

3. The bigger the idea, the more it unifies

The greatest achievements in physics are always unifications: Newton unified earthly and celestial motion. Maxwell unified electricity, magnetism, and light. Einstein unified space and time. Every great step forward shows that what looked like two separate things was really one thing seen from different angles.

The Moral of This Course

You are not studying physics to memorise formulas. You are studying it to train the habit of mind that built the modern world: look carefully, question everything, follow the evidence, and never let anyone tell you that you are the wrong kind of person to have great ideas.
