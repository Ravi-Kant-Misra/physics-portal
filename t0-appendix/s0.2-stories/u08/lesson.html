Schrödinger's Cat

"Before you open the box, the cat is neither alive nor dead — it is somehow both. Schrödinger invented this story not to describe a real cat, but to show that quantum physics leads to absurdity when applied naively to large objects."

Erwin Schrödinger: 1887–1961 Quantum Mechanics IB Theme 5 Austria

Who Was Erwin Schrödinger?

Erwin Schrödinger was born in Vienna in 1887, the son of a botanist and amateur painter. He grew up in a cultured, intellectually rich environment, learned multiple languages as a child, and studied physics at the University of Vienna. He was a broad thinker — as comfortable with philosophy and biology as with mathematics and physics.

In 1926, he published the equation that now bears his name: the Schrödinger equation. This is the fundamental equation of quantum mechanics — it describes how the quantum state (the wave function) of a physical system changes over time. It is to quantum mechanics what Newton's second law is to classical mechanics.

He shared the 1933 Nobel Prize in Physics with Paul Dirac for their contributions to quantum theory.

A Life of Exile

When the Nazis came to power, Schrödinger left Germany in protest (he was not Jewish, but was opposed to the regime on principle). He eventually settled in Dublin at the invitation of Éamon de Valera, who wanted to establish a world-class institute of advanced study. His 1944 book What is Life? — a physicist's attempt to understand biology — directly inspired Watson and Crick to look for the molecular basis of heredity, which led to the discovery of DNA structure.

The Thought Experiment

In 1935, Schrödinger published his famous thought experiment — not to celebrate quantum mechanics, but as a critique of how it was being interpreted. He wanted to show that a naïve reading of quantum mechanics leads to absurd conclusions.

Inside the sealed box
🐱 One cat
☢️ Radioactive atom
📡 Radiation detector
🔨 Hammer + vial of poison

If the atom decays, the detector triggers the hammer, breaking the vial, and the cat dies. If the atom does not decay, the cat lives. The box is sealed — no information gets out.

According to quantum mechanics, the atom is in a superposition of "decayed" and "not decayed" until it is measured. If we take this literally, then the cat (which is entangled with the atom) is also in a superposition:

Cat ALIVE
+
Cat DEAD

...at the same time, until you open the box and look.

What Schrödinger Was Really Saying

Schrödinger was NOT saying: "I believe cats can be alive and dead at the same time." He was saying the opposite. He was arguing that the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics — which says that quantum systems exist in superposition until observed — cannot be the complete story, because when scaled up to everyday objects it produces nonsense. No one has ever observed a cat in two states at once.

His thought experiment was a form of reductio ad absurdum — a philosophical argument that shows a theory leads to impossible conclusions when applied beyond its intended domain. He was challenging the physics community to do better: to find an interpretation that makes sense at both the quantum and macroscopic scales.

"I don't like it, and I'm sorry I ever had anything to do with it."

— Erwin Schrödinger, referring to quantum mechanics

The irony is delicious: Schrödinger created the equation that is quantum mechanics, and he also hated the philosophical implications of quantum mechanics most passionately. He was not alone — Einstein felt the same way. "God does not play dice," Einstein famously said.

Why the Question Matters

The cat problem is still unresolved in the deepest sense. There are several competing interpretations of quantum mechanics — and they all make the same experimental predictions, which means experiments cannot currently distinguish between them:

  • Copenhagen interpretation: The wave function collapses when measured. Before measurement, there is no fact of the matter about the cat's state.
  • Many-worlds interpretation: When the box is opened, the universe branches. In one branch, the cat is alive. In another, it is dead. Both branches are real.
  • Decoherence theory: Quantum superposition is real at the microscopic scale, but interactions with the macroscopic environment destroy superposition almost instantaneously for large objects. You never observe macro-superpositions because they decohere before you can see them.

The question of what quantum mechanics means — not just what it predicts — is still genuinely open. This is not a failure of physics; it is evidence that we are still, after a century, working on the deepest questions about the nature of reality.

Key IB Concept: Superposition

Quantum superposition means a particle can exist in multiple states simultaneously until a measurement forces it into one definite state. This is not just a quirk of small particles — it is the foundation of quantum computing, quantum cryptography, and MRI machines.

Timeline

  • 1887 Born in Vienna, Austria.
  • 1906–10 Studies physics at the University of Vienna under Franz Exner.
  • 1926 Publishes the Schrödinger equation — the cornerstone of wave mechanics.
  • 1927 Succeeds Max Planck as professor at the University of Berlin.
  • 1933 Awarded Nobel Prize in Physics (with Paul Dirac). Leaves Germany in protest of Nazi rise.
  • 1935 Publishes the Schrödinger's cat thought experiment.
  • 1940 Moves to Dublin; helps found the Dublin Institute for Advanced Studies at Éamon de Valera's invitation.
  • 1944 Publishes What is Life? — inspires Watson and Crick.
  • 1961 Dies in Vienna, age 73.
Moral of the Story

Schrödinger created one of the most powerful tools in physics and spent the rest of his life questioning what it actually meant. His cat reminds us that knowing how to use a theory and understanding what it is saying about the world are two very different things — and that the most honest scientists are the ones who admit that gap. Comfort with uncertainty is not ignorance — it is the hallmark of a genuine thinker. The questions you cannot yet answer are the most important ones to keep asking.
