Michael Faraday

"He could barely read properly as a child, had no university degree, could not do advanced mathematics — and became the greatest experimental scientist who ever lived."

1791 – 1867 Electromagnetism IB Theme 4 England

From Poverty to the Royal Institution

Michael Faraday was born in 1791 in Newington Butts, Surrey — the son of a blacksmith. His family was poor. He received little formal education, and at age 14 he was apprenticed to a bookbinder in London. For seven years, he bound books. But he also read them. He read everything he could find about science, especially about electricity. He attended lectures by the great chemist Humphry Davy at the Royal Institution, took meticulous notes, and then did something bold: he sent Davy a beautifully bound copy of his notes and asked for a job.

Davy hired him as a lab assistant. It was the beginning of one of the most remarkable careers in scientific history. Davy later said, with unusual self-awareness, that his greatest discovery was Michael Faraday.

A Complicated Relationship with Davy

Their relationship was not simple. Davy was brilliant and generous at first, but eventually grew jealous of Faraday's rising reputation. When Faraday was nominated for the Royal Society, Davy — the society's President — was the only member to vote against him. Despite this betrayal, Faraday never publicly criticised Davy. He simply kept working.

IB Physics Connection

Theme 4 — Fields: Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction is core IB content. The concept of field lines, Faraday's cage, and electromagnetic induction underpin electric generators and transformers.

Faraday's Greatest Discoveries

⚡ Electromagnetic Induction (1831)

Faraday discovered that moving a magnet near a wire generates an electric current. This is the principle behind every electric generator and transformer ever built. Without this discovery, there is no electrical power grid.

🔁 The Electric Motor (1821)

He built the world's first electric motor — a wire that rotated continuously around a magnet when current flowed through it. He was the first to convert electrical energy into continuous mechanical motion.

🌐 Field Theory

Faraday invented the concept of the field. He visualised invisible lines of force spreading through space around magnets and charges. This was revolutionary — action at a distance through a medium. Maxwell later gave these fields mathematical form.

🧲 Faraday's Cage

He showed that a conductor shields its interior from external electric fields. The Faraday cage is used today in MRI machines, microwave ovens, and to protect electronics from lightning.

"Nothing is too wonderful to be true, if it be consistent with the laws of nature; and in such things as these, experiment is the best test of such consistency."

— Michael Faraday

The Man Who Could Not Do the Maths

One of the most remarkable things about Faraday is that he could not do higher mathematics. He had no calculus, no algebra beyond the basics. He worked entirely with physical intuition and experiment. He described electromagnetic fields through pictures of field lines rather than equations.

This made him look slightly eccentric to the mathematically trained physicists of his day. But his physical intuition was correct. When James Clerk Maxwell — a brilliant mathematician — picked up Faraday's work, he translated the field lines into differential equations and produced the famous Maxwell's equations. He wrote that he was doing nothing more than giving mathematical form to what Faraday had already seen.

Faraday showed that you can arrive at profound physical truth through careful observation and deep intuition, even without formal mathematical tools. Maxwell gave that truth its language.

Timeline

  • 1791 Born in Newington Butts, Surrey.
  • 1805 Apprenticed to bookbinder George Riebau; begins reading science books.
  • 1812 Attends Humphry Davy's lectures at the Royal Institution; sends him bound notes.
  • 1813 Hired as Davy's lab assistant. Travels Europe with Davy, meeting leading scientists.
  • 1821 Demonstrates electromagnetic rotation — the first electric motor.
  • 1824 Elected Fellow of the Royal Society (Davy votes against him).
  • 1831 Discovers electromagnetic induction — the principle of the generator.
  • 1833–34 Develops laws of electrolysis; introduces terms: anode, cathode, ion, electrode.
  • 1845 Discovers the Faraday effect (magnetism affects light polarisation) — first link between electromagnetism and optics.
  • 1867 Dies in Hampton Court, age 75.
Moral of the Story

Faraday had every reason to fail. He was poor. He was not formally educated. He was not from the right class. His most important patron eventually turned against him. And yet he became one of the most important scientists in history, because he was gifted with curiosity, possessed of extraordinary experimental skill, and never stopped looking carefully at the world. Your background does not determine your destination. Your dedication does.
