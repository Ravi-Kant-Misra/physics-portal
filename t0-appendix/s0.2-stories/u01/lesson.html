Galileo Galilei

"Why was a scientist put under house arrest for looking through a telescope and seeing that the sky was not what the Church said it was?"

1564 – 1642 Motion & Astronomy IB Theme 1 & 3 Italy

Who Was Galileo?

Galileo Galilei was born in Pisa, Italy in 1564 — the same year Shakespeare was born and Michelangelo died. He was the son of a musician and was originally sent to study medicine at the University of Pisa. He never completed that degree. Legend says he watched a chandelier swing in the cathedral and noticed something extraordinary: it always took the same time to swing, no matter how far it moved. He was timing it with his own pulse. That moment of careful observation would define his life.

He switched to mathematics, became a professor, and began a lifelong commitment to one radical idea: the universe must be understood through observation and experiment, not ancient texts.

The Telescope and the Trouble It Caused

In 1609, Galileo heard about a Dutch invention — a device that made far objects look close. He built his own, improved it, and pointed it at the sky. What he saw changed history. The Moon was not a perfect sphere — it had mountains and craters. Jupiter had four moons orbiting it. Venus had phases, like the Moon. The Milky Way was made of countless individual stars.

Each of these observations was a direct challenge to the Aristotelian and Church-endorsed model of a perfect, Earth-centred universe. Galileo supported the Copernican model: the Earth goes around the Sun, not the other way around.

Galileo Galilei, c. 1636. He spent his final years under house arrest near Florence.

IB Physics Connection

Theme 1 — Motion: Galileo's inclined-plane experiments laid the foundation for kinematics (uniform acceleration, independence of horizontal and vertical motion).

Theme 3 — Waves/Optics: His telescope work connects to geometric optics and the nature of light.

The Conflict: Observation vs Authority

In 1616, the Roman Inquisition warned Galileo: do not hold or defend the heliocentric view as fact. He was obedient for 16 years. Then, in 1632, he published his Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems. The book was a conversation between three characters — one smart Copernican, one smart Ptolemaic, and one fool. The fool was called Simplicio (the simpleton) and was widely understood to be based on the Pope. It was not a subtle choice.

The Inquisition moved fast. In 1633, Galileo — now 69 years old and nearly blind — was put on trial. He was found "vehemently suspect of heresy" and forced to publicly recant his belief that the Earth moves around the Sun.

"And yet it moves."

— Attributed to Galileo (perhaps apocryphal, but deeply symbolic)

He spent the remainder of his life under house arrest at his villa in Arcetri, near Florence. Even there, he continued working. His final book, on the science of motion and materials, was smuggled out of Italy and published in the Netherlands in 1638.

Timeline of Key Events

  • 1564 Born in Pisa, Italy.
  • 1581–85 Studies medicine at University of Pisa; discovers pendulum isochronism.
  • 1592 Becomes professor of mathematics at University of Padua.
  • 1604 Studies a new star (supernova); argues the heavens are not unchangeable.
  • 1609 Builds an improved telescope; begins systematic observations of the sky.
  • 1610 Publishes Sidereus Nuncius — discovers Jupiter's four largest moons.
  • 1616 Inquisition warns him not to defend heliocentrism.
  • 1632 Publishes Dialogue comparing the two world systems.
  • 1633 Tried by Inquisition; forced to recant; placed under house arrest.
  • 1638 Publishes Two New Sciences, his masterwork on motion and strength of materials — smuggled out to the Netherlands.
  • 1642 Dies near Florence, age 77.

What Physics Did Galileo Actually Discover?

Kinematics: How Falling Objects Behave

Aristotle had taught that heavier objects fall faster. Galileo tested this with inclined planes (rolling balls of different masses down slopes so they moved slowly enough to time). His conclusion: all objects fall with the same constant acceleration regardless of mass. This directly contradicts Aristotle and sets up Newton perfectly.

Projectile Motion

Galileo showed that horizontal and vertical motion are independent. A ball fired horizontally falls just as fast as a ball dropped straight down. This is still a core IB Physics topic in Theme 1.

The Pendulum

He noticed that a pendulum's period depends on its length, not on how far it swings (for small angles). This became the basis for accurate clocks for the next 300 years.

Astronomical Observations

His discovery that Jupiter has moons was devastating to the geocentric model — if everything orbits Earth, how does Jupiter have satellites? It proved that not everything orbits Earth.

Moral of the Story

Galileo teaches us that evidence is more powerful than authority — but the courage to present that evidence can come at a great personal cost. Science moves forward because some people are willing to say "I have looked carefully, and what I see contradicts what we have always assumed." You don't need to be fearless to do science. Galileo was afraid. He recanted. But he kept working anyway, in his garden, nearly blind, under house arrest, writing the book that gave Newton his starting point. The work survived.
