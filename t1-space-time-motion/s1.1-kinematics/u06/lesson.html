Kinematics · Unit 6

Velocity-Time Graphs

Part 1: Graphs of Constant Velocity

SLIDE 1 What is a Velocity-Time Graph?

A velocity-time (v-t) graph plots an object's velocity on the vertical (y) axis against time on the horizontal (x) axis. It is one of the most powerful tools in kinematics because it lets us see both the motion and the displacement of an object from a single diagram.

  • X-axis: time, t, measured in seconds (s)
  • Y-axis: velocity, V, measured in metres per second (ms⁻¹)
Key Insight Unlike a position-time graph (which shows where an object is), a velocity-time graph shows how fast and in which direction an object is moving at every moment in time.

SLIDE 2 Reading a Velocity-Time Graph

Consider the v-t graph below. Each coloured segment is a horizontal line — this tells us the object moves at constant velocity during each interval.

Velocity-Time graph showing four segments of constant velocity

What does each segment tell us?

  • t = 1 s to t = 3 s: Object moves at +1 ms⁻¹ (slow, forward)
  • t = 3 s to t = 5 s: Object moves at +2 ms⁻¹ (faster, still forward)
  • t = 6 s to t = 8 s: Object is stationary (V = 0 ms⁻¹, on the x-axis)
  • t = 9 s to t = 12 s: Object moves at −1 ms⁻¹ (moving in the opposite/negative direction)
Rules for Reading Horizontal Segments
  • A horizontal (flat) line = constant velocity (no acceleration)
  • The higher the line, the faster the object is moving
  • A line above the x-axis = positive velocity (forward motion)
  • A line on the x-axis (V = 0) = object is stationary
  • A line below the x-axis = negative velocity (moving backwards)

SLIDE 3 The Y-Intercept: Starting Velocity

Look at the vertical (y) axis at t = 0. The point where the graph line (or its extension) crosses the y-axis tells you the starting velocity of the object.

The y-intercept gives the initial (starting) velocity u
Y-Intercept Rule The y-intercept of a velocity-time graph is the starting (initial) velocity of the object, often denoted u.
Tip If the graph starts at V = 0 on the y-axis, the object begins from rest (it was not moving at t = 0). If the y-intercept is positive, the object was already moving forward when timing began.

SLIDE 4 Area Under the Curve = Displacement

The most important feature of a v-t graph is that the area between the graph line and the x-axis equals the displacement of the object during that time interval.

Key Rule On a v-t graph: area under the curve = displacement (s) of the object.

Because constant velocity segments produce rectangular shapes between the line and the x-axis, we use the rectangle area formula:

displacement = velocity × time   →   s = V × t

The units confirm this: ms⁻¹ × s = m (metres, the unit of displacement).

Sign Convention for Area
  • Area above the x-axis → positive displacement (forward)
  • Area below the x-axis → negative displacement (backward)
  • Total displacement = algebraic sum of all signed areas
Worked example — area under each segment gives displacement; total displacement = +14 m

As given in the PPT: s₁ = 3 ms⁻¹ × 5 s = +15 m (area above x-axis, positive)

2

Segment 2 (t = 6 s to t = 9 s, V = 0 ms⁻¹):
s₂ = 0 ms⁻¹ × 3 s = 0 m (stationary — no area, no displacement)

3

Segment 3 (V = +1 ms⁻¹, giving +3 m):
s₃ = +1 ms⁻¹ × 3 s = +3 m (above x-axis, positive)

4

Segment 4 (V = −1 ms⁻¹, giving −4 m):
s₄ = −1 ms⁻¹ × 4 s = −4 m (below x-axis, negative displacement)

5

Total displacement:
s_total = s₁ + s₂ + s₃ + s₄
= +15 m + 0 m + 3 m + (−4 m)

Total displacement = +14 m
Remember Displacement can be positive, negative, or zero. It is not the same as distance. Distance is always positive (total path length); displacement can be negative if the object ends up behind its starting point.

Complete Rule Summary

  1. A horizontal line on a v-t graph = constant velocity (zero acceleration)
  2. The height of the line = magnitude and sign of velocity
  3. Line above x-axis = positive velocity (forward)
  4. Line on x-axis (V = 0) = object is stationary
  5. Line below x-axis = negative velocity (moving backward/opposite direction)
  6. y-intercept = starting (initial) velocity of the object
  7. Area under curve = displacement (s) of the object
  8. Area above x-axis = positive displacement; area below x-axis = negative displacement
  9. For rectangular areas: s = V × t
  10. Total displacement = algebraic sum of all signed areas
s = V × t   (valid for constant velocity)

Where: s = displacement (m), V = velocity (ms⁻¹), t = time (s)
