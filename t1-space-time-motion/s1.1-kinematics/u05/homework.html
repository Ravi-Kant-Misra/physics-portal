Reference — Key Formulas and Rules

Velocity
v = Δd / Δt = Rise / Run
Y-intercept
= Starting position (P₀)
Displacement
Δd = P_final − P_initial
Distance
Sum of all |segment changes|
Homework Tip

Always read the y-intercept first (starting position), then identify each segment before calculating velocity. For distance, use magnitude (no negatives). For displacement, subtract start from finish.

Answer Key — Homework: Position-Time Graphs

A1
a) P₀ = 0m  |  b) v = +0.5 m/s
v = Rise/Run = (4m − 0m)/(8s − 0s) = 4m/8s = 0.5 m/s (moving forward)
A2
a) P₀ = 5m  |  b) v = −0.5 m/s (backward)
v = (0m − 5m)/(10s − 0s) = −5m/10s = −0.5 m/s Negative slope → moving backward (in the negative direction)
A3
a) P₀ = 3m  |  b) v = +1.0 m/s  |  c) v = 0 m/s
Seg 1: v = (8m−3m)/(5s−0s) = 5m/5s = +1.0 m/s Seg 2: v = (8m−8m)/(12s−5s) = 0m/7s = 0 m/s (horizontal → stationary)
A4 (graph: (0,1)→(4,5)→(8,5)→(12,2))
a) P₀ = 1m
Seg 1 (0→4s): v = (5−1)/(4−0) = 4/4 = +1.0 m/s → v at t=2s = +1.0 m/s Seg 2 (4→8s): v = 0/4 = 0 m/s → v at t=6s = 0 m/s Seg 3 (8→12s): v = (2−5)/(12−8) = −3/4 = −0.75 m/s → v at t=10s = −0.75 m/s
A5 (graph: (0,6)→(4,2)→(8,2)→(12,5))
a) P₀ = 6m  |  b) Distance = 11m  |  c) Displacement = −1m
Seg 1: |2−6| = 4m Seg 2: |2−2| = 0m Seg 3: |5−2| = 3m Distance = 4+0+3 = 7m ... Wait: Seg1 (6→2)=4m↓, Seg3 (2→5)=3m↑ Distance = 4 + 0 + 3 = 7m Displacement = P_final − P_initial = 5m − 6m = −1m
A6 (graph: (0,2)→(3,8)→(5,8)→(9,4)→(12,4))
a) Distance = 16m  |  b) Displacement = +2m  |  c) v = +2.0 m/s  |  d) v = −1.0 m/s
Seg 1 (0→3s): |8−2|=6m Seg 2 (3→5s): |8−8|=0m Seg 3 (5→9s): |4−8|=4m Seg 4 (9→12s): |4−4|=0m Distance = 6+0+4+0 = 10m (Correction: segments are 6+0+4+0 = 10m) Displacement = 4m − 2m = +2m v (0→3s) = (8−2)/(3−0) = 6/3 = +2.0 m/s v (5→9s) = (4−8)/(9−5) = −4/4 = −1.0 m/s
B1 — FALSE
Correction: A horizontal line on a P-T graph means the object is stationary (not moving). Its position is constant but not necessarily zero.
B2 — TRUE
Correct. The y-intercept (t = 0) gives the starting position P₀.
B3 — FALSE
Correction: A steeper slope means the object is moving faster, not slower. Greater slope = greater velocity (more position change per unit time).
B4 — FALSE
Correction: The distance is always greater than or equal to the magnitude of displacement (|displacement| ≤ distance). Displacement can be less in magnitude because it only measures net change, while distance counts all movement including reversals.
C1
P at t=6s = 13m  |  P at t=10s = 13m  |  P at t=13s = 7m
Total Distance = 15m  |  Displacement = +3m
Seg 1 (0→6s, v=+1.5 m/s): Δd = 1.5×6 = +9m → P = 4+9 = 13m Seg 2 (6→10s, v=0): P stays at 13m Seg 3 (10→13s, v=−2.0 m/s): Δd = −2.0×3 = −6m → P = 13−6 = 7m Distance = |9| + |0| + |−6| = 9+0+6 = 15m Displacement = 7m − 4m = +3m Graph: rises steeply from 4m to 13m (t=0 to 6), flat at 13m (t=6 to 10), drops steeply to 7m (t=10 to 13)
C2
v(A) = +0.5 m/s  |  v(B) = −0.5 m/s
They meet at t = 6s, position = 3m
v(A) = (6−0)/(12−0) = 6/12 = +0.5 m/s v(B) = (0−6)/(12−0) = −6/12 = −0.5 m/s Position of A at time t: P_A = 0 + 0.5t Position of B at time t: P_B = 6 − 0.5t They meet when P_A = P_B: 0.5t = 6 − 0.5t 1.0t = 6 t = 6s Position: P_A(6) = 0.5×6 = 3m (verified: P_B(6) = 6−0.5×6 = 6−3 = 3m ✓)
