Average vs. Instantaneous Velocity

Understanding how motion differs between an entire journey and a single frozen moment in time.

Core Definitions

Average Speed / Velocity

The total distance (or displacement) divided by the total time traveled.

v̲ = Δd / Δt

This value describes the overall motion across an entire trip — it does not tell you what was happening at any specific moment.

Instantaneous Speed / Velocity

The speed or velocity at one specific instant in time. It does not depend on any movement outside that instant.

v = slope at a single point

Think of the reading on a car's speedometer — that is always the instantaneous speed.

Real-World Car Example

Imagine a car driving for exactly 1 hour and covering 65 km. The car did not drive at a perfectly constant speed — it slowed for traffic, sped up on the highway, etc. At one specific moment during the trip the speedometer reads 80 km/h.

Average speed
65 km ÷ 1 h = 65 km/h
Calculated from the whole trip.
Instantaneous speed
Speedometer reads 80 km/h
Only valid at that exact moment.
Average Velocity on a Position–Time Graph

Rule for Average Velocity on a P–T Graph

To find the average velocity between two points on a position–time graph:

  • Draw a straight secant line connecting the two points.
  • Calculate the slope of that line: rise ÷ run = Δx / Δt.
v̲ = (x₂ − x₁) / (t₂ − t₁) = Δx / Δt

For average speed, find the total distance traveled along the curve (not just the straight-line displacement) and divide by the total time.

P–T Graph: Average Velocity (Secant Line)

The blue dashed line is the secant line — its slope equals the average velocity.

Worked Example — Average Velocity from Graph

Two points on the curve are identified:

  • Point 1: t = 1 s, x = 4 m
  • Point 2: t = 9 s, x = 7 m

Draw the secant line and calculate its slope:

v̲ = Δx / Δt = (7 m − 4 m) / (9 s − 1 s) = 3 m / 8 s ≈ 0.375 m/s

The average velocity over this interval is approximately 0.375 m/s (the PPT reads ~0.3 m/s as an approximate graph-read value).

Instantaneous Velocity on a Position–Time Graph

Rule for Instantaneous Velocity on a P–T Graph

The instantaneous velocity at any point on a P–T curve is equal to the slope of the tangent line drawn at that exact point.

vᵢₙₛₜ = slope of the tangent line at that point
Key rule: Instantaneous speed is always positive (it is the magnitude of velocity). Instantaneous velocity can be positive, negative, or zero.

Example 1 — Steep Rising Section: Slope = +2 m/s

Steep upward slope → large positive instantaneous velocity.

Example 2 — Flat Plateau: Slope = 0

Horizontal (flat) curve → zero slope → object not changing position at that instant.

Example 3 — Steep Falling Section: Slope = −1 m/s

Downward slope → negative instantaneous velocity; speed is the magnitude (positive).

Example 4 — Gentle Rising Section: Slope = 3/4 = 0.75 m/s

Shallow upward slope → small positive velocity.

Example 5 — Peak of Curve: Slope = 0

At the peak of a P–T curve the tangent is always horizontal — the object is momentarily stationary before reversing direction.

Key Rules to Remember

Summary of All Rules

  1. Average velocity = slope of the secant line (straight line connecting two points on the curve).
  2. Instantaneous velocity = slope of the tangent line (line touching the curve at exactly one point).
  3. Instantaneous speed is always ≥ 0 — it is the magnitude (absolute value) of instantaneous velocity.
  4. Instantaneous velocity can be negative — a downward slope on a P–T graph means the object is moving in the negative direction.
  5. A flat (horizontal) section of a P–T graph means slope = 0 → instantaneous velocity = 0 → the object is at rest at that instant.
  6. At the peak of a P–T curve, instantaneous velocity = 0 (object momentarily stopped before reversing).
  7. Steeper upward slope = faster positive velocity; steeper downward slope = faster negative velocity (higher speed).
Secant vs. Tangent: A secant line cuts through the curve at two points (average). A tangent line just barely touches the curve at one point (instantaneous).
