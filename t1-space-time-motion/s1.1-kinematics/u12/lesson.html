Kinematics · Unit 12

Kinematics from Geometry

Proving the kinematic equations using the slope and area of velocity-time graphs

📄

Why Prove the Equations Geometrically?

The Big Idea

In Unit 11 we learned the four kinematic equations and how to apply them. But where do they actually come from? In this unit we prove three of the four equations directly from the geometry of the velocity-time (v-t) graph — using only the concepts of slope and area. No algebra required to derive them.

The Velocity-Time Graph for Uniform Acceleration

Whenever an object accelerates uniformly (constant acceleration), its velocity-time graph is a straight line with positive slope. The line starts above the origin because the object has a non-zero initial velocity u. As time increases, velocity increases at a constant rate — that rate is the acceleration a.

Two geometric properties we will use

Slope of the v-t line = acceleration (a). The steeper the line, the greater the acceleration.

Area under the v-t line = displacement (s). The shaded region between the line and the time axis represents how far the object moved.

Variable Color Code (used throughout this unit)

u — initial velocity
v — final velocity
a — acceleration
t — time
s — displacement

This matches the color coding used in the original diagrams throughout this lesson.

📈

Proof 1 — Deriving v = u + at from Slope

Result we will prove
v = u + at

Velocity-Time Graph — Rise over Run

The straight line has slope = acceleration a. The rise over the time interval t equals at.
1

Define the slope

The slope of a velocity-time graph is defined as acceleration.

slope = a = Rise / Run = (change in v) / t
2

Find the Rise

Rearranging: Rise = a × t. The vertical distance the line climbs over the time interval t equals at. This is labeled "at = Rise" on the diagram.

Rise = a×t = at
3

Express final velocity

The final velocity v equals the starting velocity u plus the rise the line has climbed.

v = u + Rise = u + at
Proven by geometry (slope of v-t graph)
v = u + at
△

Proof 2 — Deriving s = ut + ½at² from Area

Result we will prove
s = ut + ½at²

Area Under the v-t Graph — Rectangle + Triangle

The total area under the v-t line is the displacement s. This area splits into a blue rectangle (area = ut) and a green triangle (area = ½at²).
1

Area under v-t graph = displacement

The shaded region between the velocity-time line and the time axis equals the displacement s.

2

Blue rectangle (bottom region)

Height = u (the constant initial velocity). Base = t.

Area of rectangle = base × height = t × u = ut
3

Green triangle (top region)

Base = t. Height = at (the rise of the line, proven in Proof 1).

Area of triangle = ½ × base × height = ½ × t × at = ½at²
4

Total displacement

Add the two regions together to get the total area — which is the total displacement.

s = ut + ½at²
Proven by geometry (area of rectangle + triangle)
s = ut + ½at²
▯

Proof 3 — Deriving s = ((u+v)/2) × t from Trapezoid

Result we will prove
s = (u + v) / 2 × t

Area Under the v-t Graph — Trapezoid

The entire area under the v-t line forms a trapezoid. Its two parallel sides are u (initial velocity, shorter base) and v (final velocity, longer base). The height between them is t.

Area of a Trapezoid Formula

Area = (base1 + base2) × height / 2

A trapezoid has two parallel sides (the "bases") and a height measured perpendicular to those parallel sides.

1

Identify the trapezoid sides

The two parallel sides of the trapezoid are the velocity values at the start and end: u (initial velocity) and v (final velocity). These are the left and right vertical edges of the shaded region.

2

Identify the height

The height of the trapezoid (the distance between the two parallel sides) is the time elapsed: t.

3

Apply the trapezoid area formula

Displacement = area of trapezoid = (base 1 + base 2) × height / 2

s = (u + v) / 2 × t
Proven by geometry (area of trapezoid)
s = (u + v) / 2 × t
🔧

Equation 4 — v² = u² + 2as

Derived Algebraically from Equations 1 and 3

The fourth kinematic equation v² = u² + 2as cannot be read directly off the v-t graph as a simple area or slope — it requires combining two of the equations we just proved geometrically.

Algebraic Derivation

Combining Equations 1 and 3 to get Equation 4

Starting equations
Eq. 1: v = u + at → t = (v - u) / a
Eq. 3: s = (u + v) / 2 × t
Substitute t into Eq. 3
s = (u + v) / 2 × (v - u) / a
s = (v + u)(v - u) / (2a)
Apply the difference of squares: (v + u)(v - u) = v² - u²
s = (v² - u²) / (2a)
2as = v² - u²
v² = u² + 2as
Proven algebraically
v² = u² + 2as
✔

All 4 Kinematic Equations — Summary

4 Kinematics Equations — All Proven

✓
v = u + at
Proven geometrically — slope of v-t graph. The rise over the interval t equals at, so v = u + at.
✓
s = ut + ½at²
Proven geometrically — area under v-t graph = rectangle (ut) + triangle (½at²).
✓
s = (u+v)/2 × t
Proven geometrically — area of trapezoid under v-t graph, with parallel sides u and v, height t.
✓
v² = u² + 2as
Proven algebraically — substitute t = (v−u)/a from Eq. 1 into Eq. 3, then rearrange.

Key Insights

Slope → Equation 1
The slope of the v-t line equals a. The rise at time t is at. So the final velocity is u + at.
Rectangle Area → Part of Equation 2
The rectangle below the u-level has area ut — this is the displacement if acceleration were zero.
Triangle Area → Part of Equation 2
The triangle above the u-level has area ½at² — the extra displacement from accelerating.
Trapezoid Area → Equation 3
Viewing the whole area as a trapezoid with bases u and v gives displacement = (u+v)/2 × t, i.e. average velocity × time.

Worked Examples

Example 1

Using v = u + at — Applying the slope proof

A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 3 ms⁻² for 8 seconds. What is its final velocity?
Given (SUVAT)
u = 0 ms⁻¹ (starts from rest)
a = 3 ms⁻²
t = 8 s
v = ?
Geometric interpretation
The v-t line rises from u=0 over 8 s.
Rise = a × t = 3 × 8 = 24
v = u + Rise = 0 + 24 = 24
Final Velocity
v = 24 ms⁻¹
Example 2

Using s = ut + ½at² — Area of rectangle + triangle

An object has initial velocity u = 5 ms⁻¹ and accelerates at a = 2 ms⁻² for t = 6 s. Find the displacement using the geometric proof.
Rectangle (constant-velocity part)
Area of rectangle = u × t = 5 × 6 = 30 m
Triangle (extra displacement from acceleration)
Height of triangle = at = 2 × 6 = 12
Area of triangle = ½ × t × at = ½ × 6 × 12 = 36 m
(Alternatively: ½at² = ½ × 2 × 36 = 36 m)
Total displacement
s = 30 + 36 = 66 m
Displacement
s = 66 m
Example 3

Using s = (u+v)/2 × t — Area of trapezoid

A train accelerates from u = 10 ms⁻¹ to v = 30 ms⁻¹ over t = 20 s. Find the displacement using the trapezoid proof.
Trapezoid area
Parallel sides: u = 10 and v = 30
Height: t = 20 s
s = (u + v) / 2 × t = (10 + 30) / 2 × 20
s = 20 × 20 = 400 m
Displacement
s = 400 m
