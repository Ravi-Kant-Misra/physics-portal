Part A — Answer Key

A1: V = U + at = 0 + (10)(6) = 60 ms⁻¹
A2: a = (V − U)/t = (15 − 0)/5 = 3 ms⁻²
A3: a = (V − U)/t = (0 − 30)/10 = −3 ms⁻² (deceleration)
A4: V = U + at = 4 + (3)(8) = 4 + 24 = 28 ms⁻¹
A5: t = (V − U)/a = (0 − 20)/(−10) = −20/−10 = 2 s
A6: a = ΔV/t = (12 − 10)/1 = 2 ms⁻² (as shown in the lesson)

Part B — Answer Key

B1: v = +8 (positive), a = +2 (positive) → Same sign → Getting FASTER. The acceleration acts in the same direction as motion.
B2: v = +10 (positive), a = −4 (negative) → Opposite signs → Getting SLOWER. The acceleration opposes the direction of motion.
B3: v = −6 (negative), a = −3 (negative) → Same sign → Getting FASTER (in the negative direction; magnitude increasing).
B4: v = −5 (negative), a = +2 (positive) → Opposite signs → Getting SLOWER. The positive acceleration opposes the negative velocity.

Part C — Answer Key

C1: Acceleration is defined as a change in velocity. Velocity is a vector, so it has both magnitude (speed) and direction. A car moving in a circle has a constantly changing direction, meaning its velocity is changing even if its speed remains constant. Since velocity is changing, the car is accelerating (this is centripetal acceleration).
C2: The student is incorrect. Whether an object speeds up or slows down depends on whether the signs of acceleration and velocity match, not on the sign of acceleration alone. If v = −5 ms⁻¹ and a = −1 ms⁻², both are negative (same sign) → the object speeds up. Negative acceleration only causes slowing down when velocity is positive (opposite sign).
C3: (1) Speeding up — velocity magnitude increases; a and v point in the same direction. (2) Slowing down — velocity magnitude decreases; a and v point in opposite directions. (3) Changing direction — even at constant speed, a change in direction means velocity changes, so acceleration is occurring.
