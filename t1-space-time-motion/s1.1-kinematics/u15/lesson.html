Kinematics · Unit 15

Air Resistance on Projectiles

How air drag changes velocity, acceleration, and trajectory — including terminal velocity and the parachute effect.

Section 1 — Definition

💨What is Air Resistance?

Definition:

Air resistance is the force of air on objects moving through it. The more velocity an object has, the more air pushes back against the object's velocity.

Air resistance (Fair) always acts opposite to the direction of motion — it opposes the velocity vector at every instant. For a projectile, this means it reduces both the horizontal and vertical components of velocity continuously.

Key Rule: Fair is always opposite to the direction of the velocity vector. Gravity (Fg) always points straight down regardless of motion direction.
Section 2 — The Deceleration Pattern

⚡Newton's 2nd Law & Self-Diminishing Deceleration

Applying Newton's 2nd Law in the horizontal direction for a ball moving through air:

ΣFx = m · ax
F_air = m · a_x  (deceleration, opposing motion)

The key insight is that the deceleration pattern is self-diminishing:

Pattern: The faster an object moves, the greater Fair, so the greater the deceleration. But as the object slows down, Fair decreases — so the deceleration rate itself slows down. This creates an exponential-style decay in speed, not a linear one.

As Vx decreases, Fair decreases, so ax decreases — the ball slows down at a diminishing rate.

Section 3 — Effect on Projectile Trajectory

🎯Trajectory Comparison: With vs Without Air Resistance

Air resistance "takes away" from the velocity in both the x and y directions, fundamentally changing the shape of a projectile's path.

Without Air Resistance

  • Symmetric parabolic arc
  • Sx first half = Sx second half
  • Vx constant throughout
  • Landing speed = launch speed

With Air Resistance

  • Lower peak height
  • Shorter horizontal range
  • Asymmetric — steeper descent
  • Vx continuously decreasing
Asymmetry explained: Because Vx is continuously reduced by air resistance, the second half of the trajectory covers less horizontal distance than the first half. The descent is steeper than the ascent.
Section 4 — Vertical Motion with Air Resistance

📊Graphs of Vertical Motion (Falling Object)

Convention used throughout: Down is positive for vertical motion graphs.

For a ball dropped from rest, consider the forces acting on it: gravity (Fg) pulls it down, and as it gains speed, air resistance (Fair) pushes upward (opposing downward motion).

Graph 1: Acceleration (ay) vs Time

Reasoning: At t = 0, the ball is not moving, so Fair ≈ 0 and ay = g = 9.81 m/s². As the ball speeds up, Fair increases (opposing downward motion), reducing the net downward force. So ay decreases over time.

Graph 2: Velocity (Vy) vs Time — showing terminal velocity

Terminal Velocity: Reached when ΣF = 0 N (Fair = Fg). At this point, a = 0 m/s² and velocity is constant (maximum falling speed). On the Vy vs t graph, the curve flattens to a horizontal asymptote — its slope (= acceleration) approaches zero.
Terminal velocity condition:   ΣF = 0 N   →   F_air = F_g   →   a = 0 m/s²
Section 5 — Two-Medium & Parachute Scenarios

🌊Ball Entering Water / Skydiver Opening Parachute

When an object encounters a sudden increase in resistive force (entering water, or deploying a parachute), a new, lower terminal velocity is established. The graphs have a characteristic shape in both scenarios.

Ball Hitting Water

  • Water provides far greater drag + buoyancy than air
  • Terminal velocity in water is lower than in air
  • Vy drops sharply upon water entry
  • ay spikes negative (large deceleration), then returns to 0

Skydiver Opening Parachute

  • Parachute massively increases Fair
  • Terminal velocity with parachute is lower
  • Vy drops sharply when chute opens
  • Graph shape is identical to ball-in-water scenario
Structural parallel: Both the ball-hitting-water and the parachute-opening scenarios produce the same graph shape — a sudden spike in negative acceleration followed by a rapid approach to a new, lower terminal velocity.
