Kinematics · Unit 11

The Kinematic Equations

Predicting motion using algebra — displacement, velocity, acceleration, and time

📘

What is Kinematics?

Definition

"The branch of physics concerned with the motion of objects without reference to the forces which cause the motion."

Kinematics is the way we predict different parts of the motion of an object using only other parts of the motion. We don't need to know anything about what type of object it is or what forces are acting on it to predict its motion. We just need those other parts.

What this means in practice

If you know how fast something is going, how quickly it is accelerating, and how long it has been moving — the kinematic equations can tell you exactly where it ended up and how fast it is now going. No knowledge of mass, friction, or forces required.

The 4 Kinematic Equations

  • 1v = u + at
  • 2s = ut + ½at²
  • 3v² = u² + 2as
  • 4s = (u + v)t / 2

Each equation uses 4 of the 5 SUVAT variables. If you know 3 variables, you can solve for a 4th using the equation that does not need the 5th.

Which equation to use? — Find the missing variable

v = u + at    (no s)
Use this when displacement (s) is unknown or irrelevant. Connects initial & final velocity, acceleration, and time.
s = ut + ½at²    (no v)
Use when final velocity (v) is not needed. Links displacement to initial velocity, acceleration, and time.
v² = u² + 2as    (no t)
Use when time (t) is unknown or not required. Connects velocities, acceleration, and displacement.
s = (u + v)t / 2    (no a)
Use when acceleration (a) is unknown or not required. Works when you know both velocities and time.

3-Step Problem-Solving Method

1

Define Positive and Negative Directions

Choose one direction as positive (+) and the opposite as negative (−). It does not matter which you choose, but you must remain consistent throughout the entire problem. Any velocity, displacement, or acceleration in the negative direction must be written with a minus sign.

2

Check for Constant Acceleration

The kinematic equations only work if the acceleration is constant throughout the motion being analysed. If the object accelerates at different rates at different times, split the problem into multiple phases — one SUVAT table per constant-acceleration phase. The final velocity of one phase becomes the initial velocity of the next.

3

Use a SUVAT Table and the GUESS Method

List all known and unknown SUVAT variables in a table. Use the GUESS method to solve: Given (list knowns), Unknown (identify what you need), Equation (pick the equation with your 3 knowns & 1 unknown), Substitute (put numbers in), Solve (rearrange and calculate). If you have 3 kinematic variables, you can always solve for the 4th.

Sign Convention is Critical

When an object reverses direction (e.g. goes up then comes back down, or rolls uphill then downhill), the final velocity has the opposite sign to the initial velocity. If you defined "up the slope" as positive, then a velocity "down the slope" must be written as negative. The same applies to deceleration — if it opposes the positive direction, it carries a minus sign.

Worked Example 1 — Running Dog

Example 1

Find Initial Velocity

"A dog is running, and starts to get faster at 2 ms⁻² for 3 s. If the dog covers 20 m over this time, what velocity did it start with?"

Step 1 — Define direction: forward = positive.

Step 2 — Acceleration is constant throughout, so no splitting needed.

Step 3 — Fill SUVAT table:

suvat
20 m ? not given 2 ms⁻² 3 s
Equation Selection (E)
Known: s, a, t   →   Need: u   →   Missing from equation: v
Selected: s = ut + ½at²
Rearrangement and Substitution (S)
s = ut + ½at²
s − ½at² = ut
s/t − ½at = u
20/3 − ½ × 2 × 3 = u
6.667 − 3 = u
Answer
u = 3.7 ms⁻¹

Worked Example 2 — Ball Rolling Up a Hill

Example 2

Direction Convention & Negative Values

"A ball is rolling up a hill at 10 ms⁻¹ but gravity is causing it to slow at a rate of 1.5 ms⁻². The ball is accelerated by gravity until it is moving at 5 ms⁻¹ down the hill. How much time did this take?"

Direction convention: up the slope = positive (+)

Sign reasoning: initial velocity is up the slope (+10 ms⁻¹). Final velocity is down the slope, so v = −5 ms⁻¹. Gravity decelerates the ball (opposes upward motion), so a = −1.5 ms⁻².

SUVAT Table:

suvat
not given +10 ms⁻¹ −5 ms⁻¹ −1.5 ms⁻² ?
Equation Selection (E)
Known: u, v, a   →   Need: t   →   Missing from equation: s
Selected: v = u + at
Rearrangement and Substitution (S)
v = u + at
v − u = at
(v − u) / a = t
(−5 − 10) / (−1.5) = t
(−15) / (−1.5) = t
Answer
t = 10 s

Worked Example 3 — Multi-Phase Car Problem

Example 3

Splitting Into Multiple Phases

"A car is accelerating from rest at 5 ms⁻². It does this for 10 seconds before it stops accelerating and drives at a constant velocity for 4 seconds. It then slows to a stop at a constant acceleration in 6 seconds. What total displacement did it cover?"

Step 2 in action — Split into 3 phases

The car has three different accelerations (5 ms⁻², 0 ms⁻², then some negative value), so we treat each phase as a separate SUVAT problem. The final velocity from one phase becomes the initial velocity of the next.

Phase 1 — Speeding Up

v = 0 + 5(10) = 50 ms⁻¹
s₁ = 250 m

(a = 0, velocity unchanged)
s₂ = 200 m

(equation 4: no a needed)

Key Insight Summary

What you need to remember

5 SUVAT Variables
s (m), u (ms⁻¹), v (ms⁻¹), a (ms⁻²), t (s). Know 3 → find the 4th.
4 Equations
v = u + at  |  s = ut + ½at²  |  v² = u² + 2as  |  s = (u+v)t/2. Each omits one variable.
Sign Convention
Pick + and − directions first. Reverse direction = opposite sign. Stay consistent throughout every phase.
Multiple Phases
Different acceleration = different phase. Use one SUVAT table per phase. Add displacements at the end.
