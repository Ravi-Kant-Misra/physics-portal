Part C — Short Answers

Q11: Defining a positive direction gives every vector quantity (displacement, velocity, acceleration) a consistent sign. If you are inconsistent — for example, writing a velocity as positive when moving in the negative direction — the equation will give a wrong numerical result, because the algebra relies on signs being coherent throughout.
Q12: The kinematic equations require constant (uniform) acceleration throughout the motion being analysed. When acceleration changes value, you must split the journey into separate phases — one SUVAT table per phase. The final velocity from phase 1 becomes the initial velocity of phase 2.
Q13: No — a negative displacement does not indicate an error. It simply means the object ended up in the negative direction relative to the defined positive direction. For example, if right is positive and the object moved to the left, displacement is negative. A negative result is physically meaningful and correct.
